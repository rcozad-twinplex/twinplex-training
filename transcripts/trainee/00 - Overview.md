# Legal Requirements

When you purchased this Eyelet Training Series, you signed an agreement which allows you to use the program within your company. However, this agreement also limits you in some areas. These areas are outlined below:

1. You may not make any copies of the materials unless you need them as part of the training. Some examples to clarify this include:
    - If a person is in training and needs a copy of a Performance Check to practice on the floor — making that copy is ok. That copy must be destroyed or placed in the trainees file after completion.
    - If you have more trainees than manuals, you may not legally copy any modules or manuals. Please call us for additional copies. The cost is not that great.

2. You may not change or modify any of the materials by re-typing them to include your changes. In effect, you have purchased the right to use the materials, you do not own the materials themselves. This type of agreement is called a "*Limited Use*" License. If you have additions that outline current practice in your company, you can type up separate pages and insert those pages in the right place in the manual. These additions, again, may not contain any of the original, copyrighted material.

3. Each person who comes in contact with the Eyelet Training Series must sign a confidentiality agreement.

So why be so strict about this? Some people would say "*so what*" or "*what's the big deal? Why is maintaining the integrity of the copyright and license so important?*" Well, the reason for it is twofold:

- First of all, Trans-Matic Mfg., Co. and Lubbers & Associates have invested thousands of dollars and hundreds of hours into this program. They deserve to be treated professionally and they deserve to earn a return on their investment.

- Second, your company has made a great investment in this program. If you do not know where each manual is, your competitors end up getting the program for free. Why should you have to pay and not them? It gives them an unfair advantage.

For these reasons, we've set up a Library System which helps you organize your system. With it, you monitor who has which manual at any given time.

It is your job to monitor the process and to make sure that the copyright and license integrity is maintained.

# Roles and Responsibilities

**Role #1: Trainee/Learner**

The trainee:
- Uses initiative and perseverance to complete the program on time.
- Teams up with trainer/mentor to set time goals.
- Asks trainer/mentor: questions as required, for demonstrations when unfamiliar with how something operates, for performance check completion when appropriate.
- Monitors his/her own progress and initiates contact with trainer/mentor if difficulties are encountered.

**Role #2: Trainer/Mentor**

The trainer/mentor:
- Holds trainee accountable for progress.
- Teams up with trainee to set time goals.
- When asked by the trainee: answers questions, gives demonstrations, gives performance checks, assists in clearing obstacles.
- Has completed the training himself/herself and suggests improvements in content.
- Gives effective feedback to trainee.
- Supports the program by providing the time and materials to make it happen.

**Role #3: Facilitator**

The facilitator:
- Initiates training priorities, goals, objectives.
- Keeps records of trainee progress.
- Keeps Library System for checking out manuals.
- Monitors overall progress to goals.
- Supports trainer/mentors and trainees regarding process difficulties.
- Gives effective feedback to trainers/mentors and trainees.
- Maintains the legal requirements of the program.

**Role #4: The Program**

The program:
- Provides consistent, relevant skills and knowledge.
- Measures actual skills and knowledge.
- Duplicates on-the-job conditions.
- Provides a structured, step-by-step training process that is not dependent on any one person.

# A Few Words About This Training Program

Welcome to this training experience! As with any new program, you're probably a little apprehensive right now... and that's OK. We think you'll find this training experience different in that you won't have someone looking over your shoulder as you work through each module. As the learner, you will be responsible for your own progress.

When talking to supervisors, the question that always comes up is "*What do you look for in the people you hire?*" One of the top three responses is "*I want people with initiative.*" This program will work well for you if you use lots of initiative. In fact, the training process we use in this program will build your initiative skills. Your first goal will be to turn your apprehension into focused initiative.

Another major focus of this program is that it is competency-based... this means that you must demonstrate your ability to perform each skill before moving on.

To assist and coach you, as well as answer some of your questions, you'll be assigned a "*personal trainer*". Just like getting in shape physically, this "*personal trainer*" can't do the exercises for you. If you're going to get into great physical shape, you need to do the work... you need to set goals for yourself!! It's the same with this program... your personal trainer will assist you... they won't do the work for you. They will help you:

- [ ] Understand the training "*process*".

- [ ] Set time goals.

- [ ] By answering questions.

- [ ] By giving demonstrations.

- [ ] Monitor your progress.

- [ ] By giving performance checks.

- [ ] Your personal trainer will not tell you every move you must make. You are personally responsible for your own progress in this program.

Again, welcome to this training experience! We trust that it will be rewarding and beneficial.

# How To Use This Manual

The training method we use in this program is called "*self-paced, self-directed, competency-based training*". To accomplish this method of training, the material you find in each section follows a standard step-by-step process. This process includes:

**Step #1: Theory/knowledge.**

You will learn the basic theory and knowledge required to perform the task.

**Step #2: Written practice.**

Theory is immediately followed by written practice exercises to assist in committing this knowledge to memory.

**Step #3: Demonstration.**

You must ask for a demonstration of the skill if you feel you need one.

**Step #4: Hands-on practice.**

After the demonstration, you will practice each skill several times to become skilled at performing it.

**Step #5: Performance check.**

You will ask for the opportunity to prove your skill by performing one or more competency checks for each task. You must finish each performance check before moving on.

**General Guidelines:**

1. A trainer will be assigned to assist you. Your trainer will be available for questions, demonstrations, performance checks, setting time goals, and monitoring your progress.
2. **Do not write or make any marks in the training material**. Write answers to any written exercises on a separate sheet of paper. If you require a copy of a performance check for practice purposes, make a copy of it.
3. Use the "Checksheet" at the front of each module to track your progress. Make a copy of this checksheet before beginning the module. **Do not write on the checksheets found in the manual**.
4. Make a copy of the "Press Operator Training Matrix" to track your progress through the Press Operator Training Program.
5. Your initiative will carry you through this program. Follow your "Checksheet" to prompt you through each step of a module. Don't get caught waiting for someone else to tell you what to do next. **Just do it!!!**
6. Each manual can be checked out, library style, from the Training Director. Ask your trainer for instruction on how to check out material.

# Stages of Learning

"*First say to yourself what you would be; and then do what you have to do*".
—Epictetus

Every day must be a learning experience to truly "*do what you have to do*." However, many of us don't really understand the learning process, and as a result, we give up too early. Many of us have an early failure and we say, "*I can't do this*" or "*This isn't working*". However, if you understand the stages of learning, you'll have a much better chance of working through these early failures.

Spend a few minutes getting familiar with the "*Stages*" of learning. Chances are you can relate these stages with something you've learned in the past... playing the piano, playing ping pong, playing cards, etc. Try to visualize the stages you went through as you learned the new skill.

This training experience will follow these stages. Remember, the secret to full integration is to persevere through each of the stages.

The stages of learning include:

- [ ] **Stage One: Awareness** (unconsciously incompetent)
    - You learn there is a new and better way.
    - You might actively search for a new and better way because something isn't working.
    - Someone tells you about this training program to learn new skills for your job.

- [ ] **Stage Two: Awkwardness** (consciously incompetent)
    - You'll feel really clumsy at first.
    - Doesn't mean there's something wrong — you just need more experience.
    - Ringo Starr — "*If you want to play the blues, you gotta pay your dues... It don't come easy.*"
    - Practice, practice, practice.

- [ ] **Stage Three: Skillfulness** (consciously competent)
    - You can perform the skill well, but you need to think about what you're doing.
    - Will take a good deal of thinking and planning, but with good results.

- [ ] **Stage Four: Integration** (unconsciously competent)
    - You can perform the skills well without thinking about it.
    - The behavior becomes automatic — a part of you.

**Action Plan:**
- Be prepared for the awkwardness.
- Don't give up in the awkwardness stage — persevere through it.
- As you practice you'll get better, more comfortable, and more successful.
- The effort will be worth it!!

# Welcome To The World Of Deep Drawn Metal Stampings

Welcome to the Eyelet industry. For some of you, the words "*Eyelet*" and "*Deep Drawn Stampings*" are nothing new, since you have either been with our company for some time, or you have experience in the industry. Many of you, though, probably wonder what these words mean. Its very possible that you are either very excited about this new challenge... or you wonder what you've gotten yourself into!

Let's take a few minutes and clear up some of these questions. Some frequently asked questions, along with their answers, are given as follows:

**Q. Why is it called the "*Eyelet industry*"?**

A. The "*Eyelet*" machine was invented in the late 1800's to produce the eyelets that are used in shoes. That's where the name "*Eyelet Press*" and "*Eyelet Industry*" came from. It's actually quite remarkable that the name has held up through all these years.

**Q. So what is meant by "*Deep Drawn Metal Stamping*"?**

A. The presses we use make a great variety of parts. The generic title that some people prefer we use to describe these various parts is "*Deep Drawn Metal Stampings*".

**Q. But why not just call them "*Metal Stampings*"?**

A. We add the words "*Deep Drawn*" to separate what we do from what other stampers do. It tells our customers what business we're in.

**Q. But don't all metal stampers do the same thing?**

A. No, they don't. Let me give you an example:

> *Some people think that all farmers are in the same business. But that's not necessarily true. Some farmers raise livestock like hogs, cows, and chickens, while others raise crops like corn, wheat, and soybeans. To say that a livestock farmer is in the same business as a crop framer would not be a true statement because they are really very different businesses.*

The same is true of metal stampers. Some stamp large flat panels and brackets, some bend and shape, while we "*deep draw*" metal to make parts.

# Welcome To The World Of Deep Drawn Metal Stampings

**Q. I think I understand flat stamping to mean stamping things like brackets and car panels, but how do you recognize a "*deep drawn*" stamping? What are some of the finished products?**

A. Deep drawn stampings are in use all around us. They range from pen cases, lipstick cases, door knobs, door lock components, engine components, oil filter cases, cans, and thousands of other parts.

**Q. So how are these finished products made on an Eyelet press?**

A. Another name for an "*Eyelet Press*" is the term "*Transfer Press*". A press of this type converts a flat piece of metal into a finished, deep drawn shell. We could get real technical here, but we'll save that for later. For now, just remember that an "*Eyelet Press*" has several stations. The flat piece of metal is transferred through each station, each of which perform a different operation. A common series of operations is shown below in making a latch case. Each part produced on an "*Eyelet Press*" follows a similar progression, but uses it's own unique set of tools.

# Welcome To The World Of Deep Drawn Metal Stampings

**Q. I've heard that this business can be frustrating at times, but that it's a challenging and fulfilling career. What can you tell me about this?**

A. This is a highly skilled business since there are hundred of variables in the deep draw process. This does lead to frustration at times. A person with the right gifts and talents, along with a heavy dose of initiative and perseverance, will find this an extremely rewarding career. If you like daily challenge, a fast pace, a unique skill, and the chance for exceptional pay, this is the job for you.

**Q. You've got my attention! What do I need to do? When you say "*highly skilled*" and "*challenging*", it sounds like a lot of time and effort on my part. How do I proceed?**

A. Many people look at this business, look at the wages and status of an Eyelet Tool and Die Maker, and immediately want to become an Eyelet Tool and Die Maker.

In general, we can separate all of these people into three (3) camps:

- [ ] Those who have the gifts, talents, and potential for the job.
    - these people use their own initiative and desire to learn the skills required for this job.
    - they view each day and new challenge as a learning experience.
    - they learn with patience, but also with a "*sense of urgency*".

- [ ] Those who do not have the gifts, talents, and potential for this job.
    - All of us have unique gifts and talents. One of our true "*life*" jobs is to find something that we enjoy and that we're good at. Many people live a life of pain and stress trying to do something they don't enjoy and are not good at.

- [ ] Those who want something for nothing.
    - these people see the rewards but don't want to put in the time. They try to shortcut the system. While they might move quickly in the short term, they will ultimately fail and become part of group two (2).

**Q. So you're saying that not everyone has the right combination of gifts, talents, and potential for becoming an Eyelet Tool and Die Maker. I can relate to that. Along with this, however, how about those people who don't want the stress of that job anyway? What other career options are available for them?**

A. You'll notice three distinct job titles as you progress through this training program. They include "*Press Operator*", "*Die Setter*", and "*Eyelet Tool and Die Maker*". Each of these is an excellent career choice. You need to pick one that will keep you happy and challenged, then go out and become the best at it. Your supervisor can help you with other detailed questions you might have.

1. All written performance checks and performance check answer sheets are found in the instructors manual.
2. Collect all copies of program materials that were made and throw them away. Only the master materials in the manuals should exist.

If they don't, ask them to practice until they can. The trainee must perform the task without the Performance check for reference, unless the Performance check specifically tells them they may.
