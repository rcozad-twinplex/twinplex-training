# What is a Coolant System?

A coolant system is the system that delivers drawing compound to the punch and die area of the press. This drawing compound cools and lubricates as metal is formed in the press. To produce quality parts, it is essential that this *"coolant"* be consistently mixed and delivered to the tooling area. It is your responsibility to make sure that this happens.

# How does a Coolant System Work?

A coolant system takes drawing compound stored in a reservoir and pumps it through various hoses and lines. The coolant lines can be adjusted so that *"just the right amount"* of drawing compound is placed in *"just the right spot"* throughout the stamping process.

A coolant system is made up of several components. They include:

- [ ] The reservoir, which stores the coolant.
- [ ] The pump, which moves the coolant.
- [ ] The hoses, which carry the coolant to the coolant bar.
- [ ] The filter, which filters out solid contaminants.
- [ ] The coolant bar, into which adjustable lines are fastened.
- [ ] The coolant lines, which direct and adjust the rate of coolant flow to the tooling.

# What Type of Oil is used in a Coolant System?

The *"coolant"* used has the properties of both cooling and lubrication. For that reason, it is more accurately described as a *"drawing compound"*. You'll hear both of these terms used interchangeably at times. To avoid confusion, we'll use the word *"coolant"* to refer to any oil or drawing compound used to cool and lubricate the tooling in the stamping process.

The coolant we use can be different for each job we run. The type used for each job is sometimes listed on various places, like on the process sheet or a magnetic tag stuck to the press.

Some of the coolants are called *"water soluble"*. This means that the oil must be mixed with water. Two important things to remember are:

- [ ] **The ratio of water to oil** - the ratio is extremely important. For example, always mix the coolant to the ratio specified for the job. A ratio of 10:1 means ten parts water to one part oil.

- [ ] **The method of mixing**. Always mix the oil into the water, not the other way around. If you mix it incorrectly, the coolant could lose its properties of lubrication. This could cause severe scoring or galling of the parts.

# Coolant System Principles

You must remember some key principles about coolant systems to perform your job effectively. They include:

- [ ] **Know your coolant system.**
  We have a variety of presses, and for that reason, a variety of coolant systems as well. While they perform basically the same function, it is important to know the following for each coolant system:
  - Where the reservoir is located.
  - What coolant is used for the job currently in the press.
  - The flow rate of the coolant for the current job.
  - The location and maintenance procedures for the filter.
  - Any special instructions for each coolant system.

- [ ] **Use the correct coolant.**
  The type of coolant used for any job is specified. See your Supervisor to determine what type of coolant to use for each job and where this information can be found. If you don't know, ask.

- [ ] **Check reservoir level frequently.**
  The coolant is normally flooded into the tooling during operation. Coolant is carried out by the parts produced and the scrap generated. For this reason, always check the coolant level often - as much as every time you walk up to the press.

- [ ] **Check coolant flow rate.**
  Sometimes stuff can get into the coolant lines and plug them up. Check the flow rate of each coolant line every time you walk up to the press to make sure it remains constant.

- [ ] **Mix coolant correctly.**
  It makes a big difference how you mix some oils. Some of the coolants we use are water soluble - that is, you must mix them with water. To manage this mixing process so that you're never wrong... **Always mix the oil into the water.** Put the water in the pail first, then pour in the oil while mixing.

- [ ] **Watch your mixing ratios.**
  The mixing ratio is the amount of water to the amount of oil. A 2:1 ratio would be two parts water and one part oil. A 10:1 ratio would be ten parts water and one part oil. Always mix to the ratio specified. Use a refractometer to test your mixing ratios.

- [ ] **Watch for evaporation of water.**
  On long running jobs the water in the coolant mixture can evaporate. This leads to a thick mixture. To correct this, mix up a batch of coolant that has a much higher ratio of water. Stir this *"thin"* mixture into the coolant tank. Never mix in plain water—the coolant could lose its properties of lubrication.

- [ ] **Dump Coolant.**
  When coolant is pulled out of a press, it must be disposed of properly. Ask you Supervisor where old coolant is disposed of. Never dispose of it down a standard drain. Dumping old coolant in appropriately could lead to environmental problems and large fines.
