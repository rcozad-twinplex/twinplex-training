# Draw/Redraw Components

A draw station takes the cup, or another draw station shell, and forms it into a shell which is smaller in diameter and longer in length. A redraw station accomplishes the same purpose except that it leaves a step on the shell. The various types of draw stations include:

1. standard draw
2. step draw - creates step on part with either a spring sleeve or a stepped punch
3. redraw - uses spring sleeve to draw either a full draw or a step draw
4. finish draw - forms shell to final shape and size
5. flattening stations - these stations flatten the flange or set some portion of the shell, like the radius size or the length of a step, etc.

The draw/redraw shell is formed using the following components:

1. chuck
2. punch
3. stripper
4. die
5. knockout or bottoming knockout
6. spring sleeve (optional)
7. nest (optional)

## [Page 5]

# Draw/Redraw Components - cont.

Of special note are three items: the spring sleeve, the bottoming knockout, and the nest.

### Spring Sleeve

A spring sleeve is used in a draw station for one of two reasons:

1. It aligns the shell on the punch
   - when alignment in a draw is critical or when you are drawing a step in the shell, the shell must be perfectly aligned with the punch. The timing for this type of draw is identical to that of a standard draw.
2. It prevents wrinkles in the flange
   - when a spring sleeve is used to reduce wrinkling in the flange (most often used with very thin material), the punch is timed more slowly. Spring sleeve size, radius, finish, and spring tension become very critical in this instance.

### Bottoming Knockout

A bottoming knockout is used most often in a flattening station to stop the shell at a certain depth within the die. The one thing you must be careful of is the punch depth. Adjusting the punch too deep will cause the punch to bottom out on the knockout.

### Nest

A nest is used as the top portion of the die when you are drawing a step in the part. The shell coming into the station must fit within the nest to provide alignment.

# Mounting and Adjusting Draw Tools

The procedures for mounting and adjusting draw tools are as follows:

1. **Clean tools and press** - clean shoulder and threads of chuck and the inside of the plunger of all dirt and foreign material.
2. **Insert punch** - insert stripper dogs into stripper arm, loosen the stripper arm adjustment lock nut, and back off stripper adjustment screw. Insert punch into stripper, thread punch into plunger, and tighten securely.
3. **Jog punch into die** - jog punch down to within 1/4 inch of die surface and loosen front die bolt. Pry punch into die and snug front die bolt.
4. **Adjust punch timing** - jog press to first lift position. Loosen lifter bar and adjust punch height to create a two to three material thickness gap between the bottom of the punch and the top of the die. Center lifter bar on lifter cam and tighten bolts securely.

   *Note: Some draw stations with sleeves must have the punch in the fully down position in the first lift position. The knockout cam needs to be adjusted accordingly. Also, before you jog press, make sure the timing of the knockout is slow enough so that the punch and knockout do not lock up.*

5. **Adjust knockout cam timing** - adjust it so that the knockout is two to three material thicknesses below die surface. Tighten the bolts securely.
6. **Adjust stripper height** - jog press to stripper delay position. Using the slide sample from the station, adjust the stripper height so that the shell is carried about 1/32 off die surface. Tighten stripper arm adjustment screw lock nut securely.
7. **Make a shell** - insert shell into finger, loosen front die bolt, and jog shell into the die, stopping on bottom. Tighten front die bolt and jog press to top of stroke. Check the shell length and adjust wedge to create correct shell length. Jog one more shell through the station to double-check all adjustments.

   *Note: Shim punch if required. Also, if adjusting a flattening station, be careful you do not drive the punch in too deep.*

8. **Inspect the shell** - the drawn shell must:
   - a. Be free of score marks, rub marks, shavings, and bulges.
   - b. Have concentric flange and radii.
   - c. Be the same length as the slide sample.
   - d. Be held securely by the fingers.
   - e. Be delivered to the next station with no interruption of operation.
9. **Double-check all adjustments** - Double-check all adjustments and the tightness of all bolts.

# Correct Draw Station Problems

Draw stations can develop a number of problems. The list below gives you some common problems, a few causes for each one, and the possible solutions for each.

| Problem | Possible Causes | Solutions |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Wrinkles in the flange | a. spring sleeve spring broken | 1. replace spring |
| | b. blank cut-out | 1. see module on troubleshooting blank |
| | c. shell is drawn too deeply | 1. adjust shell to length to match slide sample; 2. balance draws - see module on final adjustments |
| | d. shell coming in is too short | 1. check length-too short readjust if necessary |
| | e. insufficient hold-down pressure | 1. increase hold-down pressure |
| 2. Shell radii larger on one side than the other | a. misaligned punch and die | 1. realign |
| | b. non-concentric radii | 1. check and on tooling re-grind or replace |
| | c. fingers misaligned with tooling | 1. shim punch and realign die |
| | d. finger spring tension | 1. readjust |
| 3. Bulge in side wall of shell/collapsed shell bottom | a. timing/adjustment of components | 1. knockout timing too fast; 2. punch timed too slow; 3. stripper set too low |
| 4. Shell tips over as slide moves forward | a. timing/adjustment of components | 1. knockout timed too fast with the friction too loose; 2. knockout timing too slow; 3. stripper set too high |
| | b. fingers do not grip shell properly | 1. check finger spring tension; 2. make sure slide guide is in place |
