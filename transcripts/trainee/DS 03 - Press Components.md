## Waterbury Farrel ICOP - Timing

Now that you can identify the components of an ICOP, it's time to talk about how it operates. You've probably been around the presses for awhile now and know that everything seems to move at once. It is critically important that you understand exactly how an ICOP operates before you move out of this section! Knowing how an ICOP operates and is timed frees up your entire thought process as you move forward. People who really understand the operation of an ICOP move forward at a much faster pace through the rest of these modules.

Having said that, now comes the hard part... actually explaining the operation and timing of an ICOP on paper. We'll accomplish this by splitting things up and with some direct hands-on experience. We'll talk about operation and timing as two separate functions to give you some knowledge before you practice on a press.

### Operation

An ICOP is a marvel to watch in production. There are many moving parts and things happening in each revolution. All of this motion begins at the motor and clutch and is transmitted through a series of shafts. Each shaft is connected by a gear to another shaft. The energy and motion from the motor and clutch is then transmitted to various parts of the press. Here is how the operation works:

**Motor & Clutch**
- generate energy and motion
- the motor and clutch is connected to the:

**Backshaft**
- the backshaft has a gear which drives the bull gear which is attached to the:

**Topshaft**
- the top shaft provides motion to the plungers through the use of plunger cams and lifter cams.
- the top shaft uses a bevel gear on its end to drive the:

**Side Shaft**
- the side shaft provides motion to the transfer mechanism through a cam.
- the side shaft uses a bevel gear on it's bottom end to drive the:

**Bottom Shaft**
- the bottom shaft provides motion to knockout cams, side motions, pressure pads, etc.

This is a general description of how motion is distributed to each component of the press. The best way to understand how it all works is to look at each shaft, see what drives it, what it drives, and what is attached to it.

## Waterbury Farrel ICOP - Timing cont.

### Timing

The operation we've just described is easy to understand compared to the timing of each station. In providing the flexibility to run a variety of different size and length parts, Waterbury created a press with a wide timing adjustment range. Once you understand how a single station is timed and adjusted at the different timing settings, you'll discover that there's nothing magic about it. It all makes perfect sense.

Given the picture at right, let's walk through some timing changes:

#### Slower timing

If you adjust the lifter arm up the lifter rod, you increase the gap between the bottom of the lifter arm and the top of the roller follower. As the press is turned over this causes the lift cam to contact the lifter arm *later* in the cycle than it did previously. This *"slows down"* the timing of the punch. At the same time, since the lifter arm is higher on the lifter rod, the plunger will not be lifted as high. Thus, when you slow down the timing of a punch it will not lift as high as it did before.

#### Faster timing

If you do just the opposite and adjust the lifter arm lower on the lifter rod, you reduce the gap between the bottom of the lifter arm and the top of the roller follower. This causes the lift cam to contact the lifter arm *earlier* in the cycle than it did previously. This *"speeds up"* the timing of the punch. At the same time, since the lifter arm is lower on the lifter rod, the plunger will be lifted higher. Thus, when you speed up the timing of a punch it will lift higher than before.

**General Timing Rule:** Slower... Lower / Faster... Higher

## Waterbury Farrel ICOP - Timing cont.

### General Timing Guidelines:

- The timing can be sped up only to a point where the lifter arm and roller follower lock up on the lift cam and plunger cam. Never allow the lifter arm and roller follower to lock up. Always check several spots for clearance through 360° of rotation because the cams are not always round.

- The timing can be slowed down only to a point where the punch is so low that the part coming into the station is knocked over. Always leave enough clearance so that the part can transfer in smoothly.

- There are other situations where timing is affected by components. Always bar the press after timing adjustments if you're unsure of timing requirements.

- The lift cam has two lifts, *"first lift"* and *"second lift"*. The *"first lift"* occurs after the plunger cam has driven the plunger downward so that the punch enters the die. *"First lift"* is defined as the dwell which must occur to allow the transfer fingers time to pick up the part. The *"second lift"* then lifts the plunger to the top of it's stroke. *"Second lift"* is the dwell that occurs when the plunger is in it's top position. Here's how it works:

  1. **Downstroke**
     - the plunger cam drives the plunger downward into it's lower position (punch into die).

  2. **First lift**
     - the lift cam lifts the plunger upward and dwells the punch just above die surface to give the fingers time to pick up the part.

  3. **Transfer back**
     - the transfer fingers are transferring back to pick up the parts.

  4. **Second lift**
     - the lift cam lifts the plunger upward to its upper position.
     - as the punch begins its upward motion during second lift, the stripper delay mechanism strips the part off the punch with a stripper.
     - at the end of second lift, the lift cam dwells until the plunger cam drives the plungers downward again.

  5. **Transfer forward**
     - during the dwell on second lift, the parts are being transferred forward to the next station.
     - when the transfer is completely forward, the whole process begins again.

## Waterbury Farrel ICOP - Pre-Set Press Components cont.

### Knockout assembly - ICOP cont.

The procedure for mounting a standard bumper and bottom cam is as follows:

1. **Clean hole** - clean hole in bed. Remove all shavings and dirt.
2. **Clean bumper** - clean bumper bushings after removing all burrs and high spots.
3. **Insert top bumper bushing** - insert top bumper bushing into bed hole from bottom. The step on the outside of the bushing must be up.
4. **Insert bottom bumper bushing** - while holding the top bushing insert the bottom bushing into the bed hole from the bottom side with the friction slot upward and forward. Push both bushings upward until bottom bushing is flush with bottom of bed.
5. **Mount bumper bushing plate** - while holding the bushings in the fully up position, place the bumper bushing plate in position, thread in the cap screw, then tighten it to torque specification.
6. **Mount kicker** - select a kicker and mount it correctly in the press so that the ball of the kicker is directly below the bed hole.
7. **Mount cam** - select a standard cam and mount it. Leave it loose for now. With the cam in the fully up position, the flat side of the cam should be in front.
8. **Insert bumper** - select the standard bumper - push bumper into bed from top end. Slide it in from the top side of the bed with the nib insert up.
9. **Insert bumper friction** - position the bumper flat to the front and insert the bumper friction. Tighten the bumper friction until there is enough tension on the bumper to hold it in position. Continue tightening the bumper friction until the proper tension is achieved. The bumper should not move downward easily with minimal thumb pressure. The proper tension is achieved when you can push down the bumper with your thumb and feel moderate to heavy pressure. The bumper should move downward easily with minimal thumb pressure.
10. **Jog press into position** - bar the press to the *"first lift"* position.
11. **Time the knockout cam** - turn the cam until it's flat side is straight up and down and the cam is in the fully up position. Tighten both cap screws simultaneously so that the bottom shaft is gripped evenly. The gap between the two halves of the cam must be the same on both ends (test this with a thin scale - the scale should slide into the gap on both ends).
12. **Adjust bumper flush with bed** - bar the press so that the knockout cam is in the fully up position. Press down on the bumper from the top. The bumper must be flush with the top of the bed when the knockout cam is in the fully up position. If it is not, remove the bumper nib and re-washer the nib to achieve the flush status.
13. **Repeat steps 1-12 for all standard bumpers.**

   **Note:** When a standard bumper is already in position, check to make sure all the components are properly adjusted and timed. Never assume that a bumper already in position is ready to go. Run through each step to double-check the following:
   - a. friction tension
   - b. chips and shavings causing bumper to hang up
   - c. kicker installed
   - d. bumper flush with bed with knockout cam in fully up position.

### Position Die Blocks

Positioning a die block assembly is a straightforward process. After all the die block assemblies for a given job are in the machine, you want them to be close to center and snugged down so that they are not accidentally bumped. The actual process of aligning them with the punch is completed as you put each punch in the press.

At the time you are ready to put the die block assemblies in the press you will have already pre-set the press and the lower unit components. The next step is to slide the die block assemblies into the machine.

As you slide each die block assembly into its proper location, you must complete the following steps:

1. **Check for, and remove, burrs from die block or bed.**
   As die blocks are placed in and taken out of a press during setup, burrs are likely to occur. Each burr on the die block or bed causes the die block to seat incorrectly in the press. This causes problems for alignment, repeatability, and the operation of the station. Remove all burrs with a fine-toothed file or with a honing stone held flat against the surface during the burr removal. The burr should be removed until the shiny spot (which indicates the high spot) is completely gone.

2. **Wipe bed and die blocks clean.**
   Before placing a die block into the press, wipe away any dirt or shavings from both the die block and the bed. If a piece of dirt or shaving is left, it will cause problems of alignment and repeatability, leading to poor operation of the station. All dirt and shavings should be wiped away with a clean shop towel.

3. **Push bumper rod down far enough to allow knockout to drop into the bumper hole.**
   Before placing the die block into the machine, push the bumper rod down about ¼ of an inch with your thumb.

4. **Slide the die block into the press until the knockout drops into the bumper hole. This gives you "rough" alignment of the die block.**
   When you placed each die into a die block you made sure that the station number on the die matched the bumper hole. Make sure that each die block is in the correct station during this step. Die block #2 should be in station #2 ... etc.

   The bottom line in this step is to roughly align the die with the bumper hole. The knockout must enter the bed without rubbing or hanging up. This step assures you that the die is roughly aligned with the punch as well.

5. **Finger tighten the front die bolt.**
   To make sure that the die block stays in "rough" alignment, finger tighten the front die bolt. This assures that the die block will stay put, even though it might get bumped or jarred during other setup activities. [Before final alignment of the punch and die, the front die bolt must be loosened.]

### Mounting The Slide

The slide mechanism in an Eyelet press works in the following manner:

As the press is jogged, the side shaft turns. The transfer cam contacts the front and back rollers, causing the transfer drive slide to move forward and back. This causes the slide to move back and forth, since it is attached to the transfer drive slide with the slide shoe.

By adjusting the rollers, you can change the position where the slide stops, both front and back stroke. The blank punch must line up exactly with the transfer block in the back stroke. The front stroke is set after the back stroke position has been established. The fingers must deliver the shells *exactly* in the center of each station in the front stroke. The front and back rollers are always left stationary. Never adjust either the front or the back roller! If you have problems, please see your Supervisor for assistance.

The procedures for mounting a slide are as follows:

1. **Place slide in press.**
   - a. Slide should slip in freely.
   - b. Insert and tighten pin, screw, or bracket.
   - c. Install slide friction. Set tension so that any slide slap is eliminated. Do not over-tighten the friction - this causes grooves to be worn into the slide.
   - d. Install front slide guide and tighten bolts.

2. **Check alignment of blank punch with transfer block:**
   - a. Apply layout dye to the last ½ inch of the blank punch diameter and allow it to dry.
   - b. Thread blank punch into plunger and tighten.
   - c. Push punch downward into the transfer block. Check the alignment of the blank punch with the transfer block using the visual method. Do not bar the press into the transfer block unless you can see that the alignment is close. If you can see that the alignment is not even close, see your Supervisor. If the alignment looks close, bar the punch down into the transfer block, and then back up again.
   - d. Determine if the layout die is scratched. If it is scratched see your Supervisor.

### Platarg vs. Waterbury Farrel ICOP Presses

Platarg and Waterbury Farrel ICOP presses are so similar that it doesn't make sense to cover things twice. If you have not fully studied the ICOP section, do so at this time. Any differences will be covered when we talk about adjusting those components in future modules.

### Full Follow cam vs. Adjustable Plunger Timing

Platarg makes presses in two styles. One uses a full follow cam process where the timing of each plunger is built into the lifter plunger cam and no timing adjustment is available. The other style has adjustable plunger timing and is very similar to a Waterbury Farrel ICOP. If you have the adjustable plunger timing or ICOP style, study the section on *"The Timing of a Waterbury Farrel ICOP"* found earlier in this module.

If you are unsure of which style Platarg press you'll be working on, see your Supervisor. Do not begin setup on a press you're unsure about.

## U.S. Baird - Pre-Set Press Components cont.

### Knockout assembly - U.S. Baird cont.

The procedure for mounting a standard bumper rod into a Baird is as follows:

1. **Clean hole in bed** - remove all shavings and dirt.
2. **Remove burrs and clean bumper** - clean bumper assembly after removing all burrs and high spots from the rod end.
3. **Mount bumper rod** - from the bottom side, insert the bumper rod end into the bed until the dowels of the mounting plate contact the bottom of the press. Wiggle the plate so that the dowels line up with their holes. Tap plate upward into position, insert cap screw, and tighten to torque specification.
4. **Test spring tension** - push down on the top of the bumper rod. It should move downward freely and spring back up as soon as you let it go.
5. **Adjust bumper rod** - adjust bumper rod so that it is flush with the top of the bed. Loosen the double nuts located beneath the mounting plate. Adjust the upper nut upward or downward to position top of bumper rod flush with bed. Hold top nut in place while tightening the other one against it.
6. **Mount knockout cam** - select a knockout cam and mount it in the machine.
7. **Mount kicker** - select a kicker, back off the kicker adjustment bolt, and mount the kicker in the press.
8. **Position knockout cam** - bar the machine until the knockout cam is in the fully up position (kicker is also fully up).
9. **Adjust kicker adjustment bolt** - turn the kicker adjustment bolt upward until it contacts the bumper rod. Holding the kicker adjustment bolt in position, tighten the lock nut.
   At this point, the kicker should be loose. It must never lock up against the bumper rod.

These are the only adjustments you need to make on a Baird bumper. Once they are set correctly, they do not have to be re-adjusted. In fact, re-adjusting it could cause the bumper to lock up.
