# Probes

## Probe Components

A probe system is a method of monitoring each shell as it travels through the press. The shell coming out of each station is detected by a probe, which sends a signal to the control unit located in the console panel. This signal illuminates a light in the panel which tells you that the shell has been detected and also holds it in memory. At a certain point in the press cycle, a cam-actuated probe sends a signal to the control unit to begin the check of this memory to make sure that each probe has sent it's signal, indicating a "found" shell. Later in the press cycle, a second cam-actuated probe sends a signal to the control unit to clear the signals in memory storage. This prepares the control unit for another press cycle.

If every sensor has detected a shell, the press will continue to run automatically. But, if a probe fails to detect a shell, the control unit sends out a signal to automatically shut off the press. The indicator light on the control panel corresponding to the probe that remains off, showing which probe failed to detect.

What are the components of the probe system?

The components of a probe system include:

1. Probe assembly
   a. feeler and arm assembly
   b. probe head
   c. fiber insert
   d. probe adaptor
   e. probe bracket
2. Probe wire
3. Probe bar
4. Cam, probes and cam
4. Control unit
   a. indicator lights
   b. bypass, toggle switches
   c. power/top/clear/reset lights
   d. power on/off toggle switch

## Mounting and Adjusting Probes

The procedures for mounting and adjusting the probe for any station are as follows:

1. **Assemble probe assembly** - thread the feeler fully onto the arm. Slide the fiber insert over the arm, slide the fiber insert into the probe head and snug the lockscrew up. Thread the probe adaptor onto the back end of the probe arm and tighten the lockscrew. Mount the bracket onto the probe head and tighten the lockscrew.

   Note: The slot in the fiber insert should be positioned at the top, to correspond to the slot in the probe head. Also, the top flange of the probe bracket should point away from the feeler wire.

2. **Mount probe in press** - loosen the mounting screw located on the bed, insert the probe bracket behind the washer, and snug up the mounting screw. Position the probe exactly on the spot where two stations meet and adjust it so that the probe wire is centered between the fingers and hits only the shell as it passes by.

   Note: The feeler wire may have to be shortened so that the probe can be positioned low enough to clear the strippers and high enough not to touch the bed. Remember, the probe itself must not touch the strippers, the fingers, or any other object. The feeler must be the only component of the probe to touch something, namely, the shell.

3. **Mount probe wire** - turn power switch for probe system to the on position. Turn toggle switches on at each station where a probe will be connected, and off at the others. Take a scale or small screwdriver and spread the end of the banana plug open so that the banana plug will fit tightly in both the probe bar and the probe adaptor. Insert one end of the banana plug into the correct location on the probe bar. Touch the other banana plug to a press part to check it's operation. The indicator light on the console panel should go on. Insert the loose end of the probe wire into the probe adaptor.

4. **Make final adjustments to probe** - turn the power switch for the probe system off, then back on. The indicator lights should all be off. Jog the press, without a shell, through one revolution. The probe must not show anything. If it does touch, the indicator light will go on. Adjust the probe as needed.

5. **Make a shell** - jog a shell through the station. The indicator light should go on as the probe feeler touches the shell.

6. **Mount the other probes** - mount all the other probes in an identical fashion. After the probes are mounted, the feeler wires must all touch the shells at the same time. The indicator lights must all come on simultaneously.

   Note: If the reset light comes on, it means that a probe is touching something other than the shell and grounding out. This condition must be corrected before continuing.

## A word about probe adjustment:

The temptation you must overcome while adjusting a probe is the one which tells you that it's quicker to bend a probe into position than adjusting it. A probe has four adjustment points:

- the top lockscrew to move the arm in and out and to turn the feeler
- the probe bracket lockscrew which swivels the probe
- the length of the feeler wire
- the probe bracket mounting screw to adjust the entire unit up and down and side to side

Use these adjustment points to adjust the probe. Don't turn the probe into a pretzel by bending it around.

## Correct Probe System Problems

The probe system can develop a number of problems. The list below gives you some common problems, a few causes for each one, and the possible solutions for the causes:

| # | Problem | Possible Causes | Solutions |
|---|---------|-----------------|-----------|
| 1 | Indicator light does not go on | a. burnt out bulb | a. 1) replace bulb |
| | | b. probe not touching shell | b. 1) re-adjust probe; 2) find out where shell went |
| | | c. bad probe wire* | c. 1) replace probe wire |
| | | d. probe wire not tight in probe bar or probe adaptor | d. 1) expand banana plugs so that they fit tight |
| | | e. bad relay* | e. 1) replace relay |
| 2 | Indicator light stays on | a. probe grounding out | a. 1) clear shavings; 2) re-adjust probe |
| | | b. probe wire grounding out | b. move the wire so that it does not cross another wire or touch a press part |
| | | c. bad relay | c. replace relay |
| 3 | Indicator lights do not go on together | a. probes not timed together | a. adjust probes to contact each shell simultaneously |

\*A bad probe wire can be found by replacing it with another wire. If the indicator light then works, the wire is bad. If it still acts the same, it is not the wire. A bad relay can be found in similar fashion.

# Completing The Setup

## Perform Mini-test Run

### What is a mini-test-run?

A mini-test-run is a short run that you make to determine if the press is ready to run and the shell is in spec. A mini-test-run is normally a run of about 500 to 2000 pieces.

### What must be done prior to the mini-test-run?

For the most part you have completed the entire setup. There are two specific things which must be done before you begin the mini-test-run. They include:

1. **Double-check the tightness of all bolts.**

   Go through the entire press and check every bolt to make sure it is tight.

2. **Attach and/or close all press guards.**

   There should be no gap in the press guarding where you can get your fingers in. The rule of thumb is no opening larger than two inches is acceptable. Where a pinch point is close to the guarding, no gap may occur. If any guard or guard door is opened, the press must shut off.

### What steps must be taken to complete the mini-test-run?

The following steps must be taken to run the mini-test-run:

1. **Turn the coolant on** - turn the coolant on and direct the flow onto the blank and into the tooling as called out on the setup documentation sheet.
2. **Adjust shell to specification** - make final adjustments to shell.
3. **Run press** - turn the press on and look for problems in the operation of the tooling, the press and in the shell.
4. **Check probe system operation** - pull the probe wire out of each probe in turn. The press should stop after each probe wire is pulled.
5. **Check guard operation** - the press must stop when a guard door is opened.
6. **Make final adjustments to shell.**
7. **Solve all problems as they occur.**
8. **Keep the last 10 parts of the mini-test-run for your first piece approval.**
9. **Stop the press after you are confident that the press will run quality parts on a consistent basis.**

10. **Perform first piece approval** - measure/gage/check every dimension on the shell and document on the first line of the inspection sheet. Document all findings on charts and process log. Gain approval from your Team Leader.

    **Do not fight to run poor quality - fix the problem!**

## Update Setup Documentation

### What form is used to update setup documentation?

If you remember from an earlier module, the setup documentation sheet is used to pre-set the press and the tooling for the job being set up. At this point, however, we need to update that information at the end of our job run. The form we use is called the documentation data form.

### How does the documentation data form work?

Because the job has just run, we need to take final measurements before the job is pulled. This will enable us to pre-set the tools to exactly the same position the next time we set this job up in this press. There is no magic to this form. You must measure each item found on the documentation data form and record your findings in the correct location on the form. You must turn this completed form in to the pre-setup team after completion.

### What happens to the data I collect?

The information you list on the documentation data form is reviewed by the pre-setup team. They update the setup documentation sheet so that it is ready for the next run.

## Tooling Problems and Replacement Frequency

### What is meant by tooling replacement frequency?

Tooling replacement frequency refers to:
1. identifying what tools were replaced during a job run
2. identifying how many of each tool was replaced during a job run

### Why is this important?

This information is important for two reasons:

- it identifies what spares may need to be ordered
- it identifies possible problem areas

### How do I order spares?

First, you identify how much of each piece of tooling you have; second, you identify how many of each you need; third, you identify how many you used, fourth, you identify what to order based on this information; fifth, you fill out a tool room work order or leave a note for the pre-setup team which lists what you need. They will then order it for you.

### How do I identify problems based on tool replacement frequency?

When it comes right down to it, tool replacement frequency can only identify a possible problem. You need to determine if it is a problem. The reason for this is that there are no hard and fast rules for replacement. In some cases, replacing 3 pieces of tooling may not be a problem, while in other cases, replacing just one may be serious. The only way to find out if it is a problem is through investigation. You must look at:

- number of parts run by the tool
- the vulnerability of the tooling (a long, skinny punch may break frequently while a large one should never break)
- why the tooling was replaced (normal wear or for reasons of mis-adjustment, mis-alignment, or other assignable causes)

The secret here is to try to identify assignable causes, then seek to improve or eliminate them to improve tool life.

### How do I identify what the assignable causes are for both tool replacement and all general problems?

At this point you must become a detective. The best source is the process log sheets. They should have a complete record of everything that happened on the job during the run.

### Once I find out what problems occurred, what do I do?

There are two things you need to do. First, you need to inform the team leader about what you found and second, assist in any way you can in fixing the problems before the next run.
