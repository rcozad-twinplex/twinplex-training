# Trim Components

A trim station removes the excess material from the flange of the shell. A trim station can take on one of two forms:

- **Pinch trim** — removes the entire flange, leaving a straight wall on the shell.
- **Flange trim** — removes only the excess material in the flange, leaving a flange on the shell.

These two are the most often used. In a few cases, you might find a shell with a combination pinch and flange trim.

The trim station is made up of the following components:

1. chuck
2. punch (solid punch, punch and pilot, or punch, nib, and pilot)
3. scrap removal device
4. die
5. knockout

## [Page 5]

# Trim Components - cont.

A scrap removal device is a unit which carries the trim ring out of the press. There are four basic trim scrap removal devices. They are:

1. trim horn
2. solid plate with blower
3. finger method
4. transducer

1. **Trim horn** — a trim horn allows the trim rings to stack up on the punch, eventually contacting a wedge which splits the trim ring. The trim ring is then spread apart and pushed up and over the punch. After it springs past the punch it closes again over a pipe which carries it to the scrap barrel. Since this unit is built into the punch, no special alignment or setup must be done after the punch is assembled in pre-setup.

2. **Solid plate with blower** — this device uses a solid plate to strip the trim ring off the trim punch. A blower then carries the trim ring into a chute by means of a blast of compressed air. This is the method explained in the setup procedures in this module.

3. **Finger method** — this method uses the last pair of fingers in the job to exit the trim ring. It is used only when the trim is the last station in the press.

4. **Transducer** — this method uses vacuum pressure to suction the trim ring out of the press. It is used only in those cases where the other three methods will not work and is used more often in a Baird than in an ICOP. The transducer is typically a unit which is mounted and doweled to a die block. This means that little adjustment is needed.

# Sharpening Trim Station Components

## Sharpening Standards

### Pinch trim

The pinch trim nib and die cutting edge must be sharp. The pinch trim die must have a .010 radius. This radius provides a lead in which allows the shell to enter the die without scoring or tipping.

**Note:** The nib for a pinch trim must have a cutting edge break of .005 max.

### Flange trim

The flange trim punch and die are always sharp. A slight taper (similar to the one on a pinch trim punch) can be placed on the punch if the trim rings are not tight on the punch.

### Sheared

The term "sheared" means that a cutting edge no longer produces a clean cut edge. A sheared tool will produce a shell with a burr on it. Many times, a sheared edge is caused by:

- excessive tool wear
- chipped edges on the tools
- misalignment of the punch and die
- shell tipping over and crunching between punch and die

The bottom line is that the shell has a burr on it caused by dull, rounded, or rolled over edges on tools.

There are two good ways to tell if the tools for any cutting station require sharpening. They include:

1. The shell - look for excessive burrs
2. The tools - look for dull, rounded, or rolled over edges

## Sharpening Procedures

### Punch

1. **Remove insert** - remove the screw holding the insert and pilot in the punch and remove the insert from the pilot.

2. **Grind insert** - remove all burrs on the O.D. of the insert and clamp it in a V-block. This set up will keep the insert square. If the insert is square, another method is to set the insert on the surface grinder and block it up using a pair of thin V-blocks after which you grind the top surface.

3. **Remove burr** - using a flat stone, held firmly against the O.D. of the insert, remove the burr at the cutting edge.

## [Page 10]

# Sharpening Trim Station Components - cont.

4. **Grind angle on insert** - radius or angle the inside corners of the insert to a point where the pilot shoulder seats against the insert. If a gap exists between the insert and pilot, pulling up could occur.

5. **Reassemble punch** - reassemble the insert on the pilot, and the pilot and nib assembly into the punch, using the proper length screw.

### Die

1. **Grind die** - set up die on surface grinder and remove material in multiples of .015 until the sheared area has been removed. Demagnetize die before continuing.

   **Note:** Shims used are in thicknesses of .015 and .030.

2. **Cut back shoulder** - set up the die in a lathe and use a dial indicator in the I.D. of the die to true it up. Cut the shoulder back on the step so that it corresponds to the print dimension. Break all sharp corners.

# Mounting and Adjusting Trim Tools

The procedures for mounting and adjusting trim tools are as follows:

1. **Clean tools and plunger** - clean shoulder and threads of chuck and the inside of the plunger of all dirt and foreign material.

2. **Mount punch** - remove stripper arm. Insert the punch into plunger and tightened securely.

3. **Pry punch into die** - jog punch down to within 1/4 inch of the die surface and loosen front die bolt. Pry the punch downward until cutting edges are about 1/32 of an inch apart. Loosen the chuck and turn the punch into the die without shearing the cutting edges. Pry the punch fully down and then tighten the chuck securely. The punch/nib should still be in the die.

   **Note:** If the trim is non-round, change this to read "leave punch tight, loosen plunger friction and carefully pry punch into the die without shearing the cutting edges. Double-check alignment using layout dye.

4. **Align punch and die** - align punch and die either with shims or visually. Tightened both front and back die bolts securely.

5. **Set punch depth** - jog the punch to the fully down position and adjust punch so that it enters die about 1/16 of an inch.

6. **Adjust trim timing** - jog press until the fingers are beginning to move back and will clear the trim ring. Loosen the lifter bar and adjust punch to the point where the punch cutting edge is in the fully down position. Center the lifter arm on the lifter cam and tighten it securely.

   **Note:** If you use the finger method to remove the scrap, change this step to read: jog punch to the bottom of it's stroke with the fingers fully back. Loosen lifter bar and adjust punch to fully bottom position. Center and tighten lifter bar securely. (Plunger friction, must be tight enough to keep the punch from going deeper than it is set.)

7. **Adjust knockout cam** - jog press until the fingers are fully back and the punch clears the incoming fingers. Loosen the knockout cam and adjust it so that the knockout contacts the part. Tighten both bolts securely.

8. **Mount and adjust stripper plate** - jog punch to the top of the stroke. Mount stripper plate, leaving it loose. Pry punch into stripper plate opening and jog punch to the bottom of it's stroke. Lift the stripper plate to within 1/8 inch of contacting the bottom of the chuck. Center the opening around the chuck and tighten securely. Mount blower so that the blower pipe is slightly under and centered on the stripper plate. Tighten securely. Jog press to top of stroke.

   **Note:** Make sure to jog slowly watching for problems like the fingers hitting the stripper plate. All components must operate freely.

## [Page 12]

# Mounting and Adjusting Trim Tools - cont.

9. **Make a shell** - insert a shell and jog the press slowly through one revolution. The punch should begin lifting just after the fingers are far enough back to clear the trim ring, yet the trim ring must clear the fingers coming in. The shell must be exited from the die when the fingers are fully back and the punch is clear of the fingers. Adjust the punch and knockout until these two conditions are met.

   **Note:** This adjustment of timing is for a pinch trimmed shell. If a flanged trimmed shell is exited under the fingers, it will either crunch the shell or break the fingers. A flange trimmed shell punch timing remains about the same but the knockout cam is timed faster, to exit the shell so that it follows the punch upward.

10. **Inspect shell** - (a) trimmed shell is free of score marks, shavings, bulges, and burrs, (b) edge of trim has even break, (c) shell is the same length as the slide sample and is held securely by the fingers, (d) shell is delivered to the next station with no interruption of operation.

11. **Double-check all adjustments** - double-check all adjustment and the tightness of all components.

# Correct Trim Station Problems

Trim stations can develop a number of problems. The list below gives you some common problems, a few causes for each one, and the possible solutions for the causes:

| Problem | Possible Causes | Solutions |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Flange shell has bent flange | a. shell catching under fingers | a. 1) re-time punch and knockout |
| | b. flange not protected from fingers | b. 1) adjust finger spreader; 2) re-time punch and knockout |
| 2. Wrinkled or cut-out flange | a. blank cut-out | a. 1) refer to blank troubleshooting section |
| | b. flange too small | b. 1) eared material; 2) material too thin; 3) re-balance draws |
| 3. Bulge in side | a. shell catching under fingers | a. 1) re-time punch and/or knockout |
| | b. punch, shell, and fingers do not line up | a. 1) realign |
| 4. Shell tips over as slide moves forward | a. shell not fully up | a. 1) re-time knockout and/or punch; 2) double-check knockout flush with top of die with knockout cam in fully up position |
| | b. shell pulling up | b. 1) check relationship of finish draw and trim station shells and variables; 2) re-time knockout and/or punch |
| 5. Pinch trimmed shell pulls up | a. relationship between finish draw and trim | a. 1) refer to shell procedure on finish draw and trim relationship |
| | b. timing of punch and knockout | b. 1) re-time |
