# Tooling vs. Press Components

The *"tooling for a job"*, and the *"press and it's components"*, are two different subjects. We've separated them into two different modules so that you can spend time with each of them separately.

What's the difference between them? The tooling for a job is unique to that particular job, while the press and it's components are typically used on every job you run. So when you're talking about setting up a job, you're really talking about placing the tools for the job in the press and adjusting them and the various components of the press to produce the part you want. In this section we'll look at *"job tooling"*, the tooling that is pulled out and replaced each time the press is set up for a different job.

# Tool Identification

Each job is made up of several different stations, each of which performs a different task. As you look at each of the modules in this manual you'll see some different operations like blanking, cupping, drawing, trimming, piercing, etc. Each of these stations perform a different function, but each of them uses the following tools:

- A punch
- A die
- A thimble (or stripper)
- A knockout
- A punch holder ( or chuck)
- A die block (or holder)
- A set of transfer fingers

These are the standard tools used for each station. While we'll be looking at the specific tools by station in later modules, it's important to be able to *recognize* each of these for use in this module. Let's take a quick look at each of these standard tools:

### Punch
The punch is the tool used to shape the inside of the part. It is usually attached to the upper portion of the press through the use of a punch holder. The press creates punch movement down and into the die to form a part.

### Punch Holder (or chuck)
The punch is fastened to the spindle or the ram of the press by means of a punch holder.

### Die
The die is the tool that shapes the outside of the part. It is usually attached in a fixed position to the lower portion of the press through the use of a die block or holder.

## [Page 5]

# Tooling Identification - cont.

### Die Holder (or die block)
The die is fastened to the bed of the press by means of a die holder.

### Thimble (or stripper)
When a part is formed around the punch it needs to be removed from the punch so that the transfer fingers pick it up. The thimble performs this function.

### Knockout
When a part is formed in the die it needs to be removed from the die so that the transfer fingers pick it up. The knockout performs this function.

### Transfer Fingers
Each station has a pair of transfer fingers which pick up the part and deliver it to the next station.

While this is a simplified explanation for the tooling in a press, these tooling components are standard to most stations. What you will find, however, is that each of these standard components can look very different, given the job and press.

# Tooling Location

Before setting up a job in a press, you must be able to locate the tooling components for that job. Go to your Supervisor and ask them to explain the process of locating the tools for any job.

# Tooling Inspection

After locating the job tooling and before this tooling is placed into the press, the *"Tool Control"* Department must inspect each piece of tooling for two reasons:

- To ensure that they will produce a part to blueprint specification.
- To ensure that they are within the required wear tolerance.

Some of the tooling will directly affect the finished part specifications and it's obvious that these tools be maintained within certain sizes. There are other tools that don't directly affect the part specifications. You must maintain these tools as well because they can cause other difficult to find problems. The best prevention here is to maintain all tooling to specifications. Each piece of tooling should have it's own wear allowance standards.

As you measure each piece of tooling, compare each dimension to both the blueprint dimension and also to the wear allowance. Ask your Supervisor for any wear allowance standards by which to make your comparisons.

If you find that the tool does not fit within either one or both of these tolerances, you must make a decision to replace that piece of tooling or change the book to reflect the change to that piece of tooling. The basis for making either one of these decisions are as follows:

### Replace the tooling, only if:
- Tool is worn
- No tool change improvements were made during the job run
- You have investigated all problems that might be created by replacing it
- You have approval of your Supervisor

### Change the Job Prints, only if:
- Tooling is not worn
- Tool change improvements were made during the job run
- You have investigated all problems that might be created by changing the book
- You have approval of your Supervisor

**Note:** You must anticipate that some problems may be created by either of these decisions. For that reason, never replace tooling or change the job prints without the approval of your Supervisor.

## [Page 7]

# Tool Inspection - cont.

So what if the dimension you've measured is less than the lower specification limit for the blueprint dimension? How do you really know if it should be replaced? By collecting facts. These facts include:

1. Does the tool show excessive wear in the working area?
2. Has the tool run a large quantity of parts? (this may be hard to prove)
3. Does the slide samples for the previous run measure up to the master slide samples (if available)?

The answer to these questions will help you choose whether or not to replace the tool. As we've said, replacing the tool may cause other problems. For this reason, the best way to replace a piece of tooling is immediately after the setup, before the job is pulled from the press. This allows you to fix any problems while the tooling is in the press. Why would you want to do this? It is better to gradually introduce a tooling change into a job and immediately see and fix the problems caused by the change, than to change the tooling outside of the press and try to resolve the problems during setup.

And what if the actual dimension of the tool is larger than the upper specification limit for the blueprint dimension? This might indicate that an improvement was made, and the print may need to be updated.

Before making a print change, ask yourself these questions:

1. Who changed the tool?
2. Was the change made to improve the tool?
3. What happened as a result of the change?... Does the job run better? Are the parts of higher quality?... Did something improve as a result of the change?...
4. Did anything bad happened as a result of this change?... Did the change create problems elsewhere in the job?...

The answer to these questions will help you choose whether or not to change the print to reflect a change in the tooling. Collecting facts from the person responsible for the job and from the process log records will help you make a good decision.

A decision to change the job book is a difficult decision to make. <u>Never</u> change a tooling print without approval from your Supervisor.

# Tooling Assembly

### Assembly Guidelines

Assembly is needed for many of the tools and components of a job. The guidelines for assembling include:

1. The cap screw or fastener must thread into the tool or component a minimum of 1½ times it's diameter.
   - A ½ inch screw must enter ¾ of an inch.
   - A quick way to check this is to put in the fastener and measure the amount that it sticks out, before assembly.
2. The cap screw or fastener must be torqued to specification. Never over or under tighten. If you don't have torque specs, have someone demonstrate over and under tightening.
3. Mating surfaces must seat squarely against each other. Reasons for lack of seating squarely include:
   - Radii too large
   - Tapered or non-square shoulder
   - Counterbores too shallow
   - Shank too long
4. The hold down keys and/or fasteners must clamp the tooling tightly.
5. While inserting one unit into another, they must assemble smoothly, without excessive hammering. A soft face hammer must <u>always</u> be used.
6. Where there is more than one fastener, each must be tightened simultaneously. This gives you an even grip.
7. Dies must always fit flush, within .001, of the top and bottom of the die block.
8. Use a dial indicator to align flats on odd shaped punches and dies.
9. Springs must be of the correct wire diameter, spring diameter and spring length. Whenever possible, the ends of the spring should be crimped shut.
10. Double set screws should be used whenever a set screw is used to fasten.
    - The two set screws must be locked together to prevent them from backing out.
11. Use each piece of tooling or component in the station it is designated for.
12. When a station or assembly is unfamiliar to you, always look at the cross-sectional layout and the detail print for that station or assembly to find out how it goes together.
13. All nicks, burrs, and gouges must be removed before assembly.

Each of these guidelines can be used when assembling most tools and components. Two items deserve special attention, however. They are transfer blocks and fingers.

## [Page 9]

# Tooling Assembly - cont.

### Transfer Block (or carrier block)

Each time a transfer block is mounted in the slide, two conditions must be met in addition to those already given:

1. It must have the correct inside diameter size
   a. Measured across the land at the bottom.
   b. Measured after the transfer block is mounted in the slide, with the cap screws tightened.
      - the inside diameter of the block could close up if the outside diameter is too large.
      - the inside diameter could open up after you tighten up the cap screws if the outside diameter is too small.
   c. must hold the blank tightly without distorting it.
2. It must be flush with bottom of slide.
   - transfer block must be within .003 of flush with bottom of slide.
   - if the transfer block sticks out too much, it will lock up the slide by being pinched between the blank die retainer and bed.
   - if it is too short, the blank could slip under the slide when transferred.

### Fingers

Each time fingers are mounted in a slide, additional conditions must be met:

1. When using a transfer block, the fingers marked #1 are placed in the first finger position. This means that the number stamped on the fingers do not necessarily match the station number.
2. The shell must be gripped firmly and in the correct location. Use a slide line-up plate and slide setup plugs for insertable fingers. There are setup plugs for each job which fit into the line-up plate and allow you to adjust each pair of fingers to center.
3. The two springs used for each station (front and back) must be identical in wire diameter, O.D., and length.
4. The fingers must move freely with the mounting screws, springs, and spring tension screws in place.

# Tooling Layout

Laying out the tooling means placing each tooling component on a tooling cart ready to wheel out to the press. Use the performance check to practice tool layout. Ask your Supervisor for any special instructions.
