# What is Inspection?

Remember our day-care example from the housekeeping module? What we were really doing was inspecting whether or not each day-care facility measured up to our standards. The final outcome of that inspection was a decision "*Is this the day-care we want to use?*"

Your job as an inspector is identical to this example. As you inspect the characteristics of each part, you will be asking yourself the question, "*Is this part good enough for our customer?*", or "*Does this part meet the specifications required by our customer?*" To answer these questions, you compare each characteristic to the standard, usually found on a blueprint. You accept the parts if all the characteristics meet the standard. If they don't meet the standard, you don't accept them.

Your job as an inspector involves several steps. They include:

**Step #1: Interpret specification.**
Interpret the specification as found on the blueprint or operator instruction sheet.

**Step #2: Measure characteristic.**
Measure the characteristic by variables or attributes.

**Step #3: Judge conformance.**
Compare the measured characteristic with the specification required and judge whether or not the characteristic conforms to the specification.

**Step #4: Take action.**
Make a decision and respond in an appropriate way.

**Step #5: Document data.**
Record measurements, actions taken, etc. for future reference.

This process happens very quickly and must be repeated for every characteristic on every part running in each press.

# Why is Inspection Important?

The importance of inspection can be examined best by turning it around and asking, "*What are the results of poor inspection, or no inspection at all?*" Some possible answers include:

- **Safety** - many parts are used in high profile areas like safety. What happens if the parts we make for safety devices, brakes, engines, and electrical systems fail? Fire, accidents, explosions, and other life threatening situations can be created because someone failed to inspect a part thoroughly.

- **Credibility** - if our customers are finding bad parts because we didn't inspect properly, they could take one or more actions. They could return all the parts to us for sorting, pull the job and give it to someone else, or they could refuse to give us new work. The more often this happens, the lower our credibility becomes with the customer.

- **Pride** - it should be a great source of pride to provide "*defect free*" parts to our customer. The more bad parts that get to the customer, the lower our sense of pride becomes. This results in people who "*don't care*", simply because it happens so often. Do you want to work for a company where people don't care?

It's easy to see why inspection is important once you look at it closely. It affects our livelihoods and our jobs. I challenge you to take great pride in making good decisions as you carry out your inspection tasks. Treat inspection with the seriousness it deserves.

# Inspection Steps

Inspection is a process that involves several steps. We listed these steps before, but we need to spend a few minutes on each one so that you can effectively carry them out.

**Step #1: Interpret specification.**

The first thing you need to do is to figure out what the blueprint calls for. Most of the dimensions you'll see on a print are variable data - things that can be measured with a measuring instrument such as calipers, or micrometer. A dimension of 1.000 ± .010 is fairly easy to interpret. If you measure the dimension between .990 and 1.010, the characteristic is within specification.

Attribute data can be a little more difficult - they are either there or they are not. A set of go/no-go ring gages measuring the same 1.000 ± .010 dimension is easy at first glance - the go ring (1.010) must slide over the part, the no-go (.990) should not. The difficulty comes when the dimension is near the high (1.010) or low (.990) end. Must the part fall freely through the go gage or can it drag? How about if the no-go gage starts on the part, but stops part way?

How about another example. Let's say the print calls for no score marks. You look at the part and it has some light lines beginning to form. Is the part good or bad?

# Inspection Steps - cont.

As you can see, interpreting specifications brings with it a degree of judgement. You'll find that some people have good judgement and some do not. What separates them? The people who interpret specifications accurately when inspecting use some common techniques. These include:

- **Refer to the operator instruction sheet.**
	All the information you need is on this sheet, including what dimensions and attributes to inspect, what inspection technique to use, what the specification is, etc. Use this sheet as your Bible during your inspection.

- **Study the blueprint.**
	Spend some time with the blueprint to get familiar with the part and what the specifications are.

- **Use master samples:**
	Refer to a master sample when you're in doubt about a specification. A master sample is a part that was approved by the customer and/or is known to be good.

- **Past experience.**
	Find out what's been acceptable in the past. Be careful here, though. Just because something was accepted in the past is no guarantee that it will be accepted in the future.

- **Ask.**
	Ask your team leader or supervisor if you have questions about any specification.

Interpreting specifications accurately is the first step you take when inspecting any part. Without knowing what is required you won't be able to make good inspection decisions.

**Step #2: Measure characteristic.**

The second step is to measure the characteristic. This is normally done by one of the following methods:

- A measuring instrument, like calipers or micrometer.
- A gage of some sort, like ring gages, plug gages, fingernail gages, or height gage.
- Visually, like checking for score marks or the presence/absence of some feature like a hole or slot.

We'll cover some of these measuring instruments a little later. For now, here are some important techniques for measuring.

- **Be accurate.**
	To maintain accuracy, follow these tips:
	- Clean part thoroughly before measuring
	- Avoid errors due to heat, dirt, burrs, wear, manipulation, etc.
	- Use calibrated tools
	- Use the correct measuring tool
	- Use good measuring techniques
	- Follow the inspection plan

# Inspection Steps - cont.

- **Be consistent.**
	Measure the parts the same way each time.

- **Use good technique.**
	Avoid errors of technique. Know how to use each measuring tool and device.

- **Avoid inadvertent errors.**
	Stay away from errors that result from boredom, fatigue, repetition, etc.

- **Avoid bias.**
	Don't be influenced by outside factors or getting measurements you wanted to get when measuring.

- **Know what you're looking for.**
	Most rejections are caused by visual problems (defects you can see by visually inspecting the part). Visual checks are a major portion of your inspection job. Avoid visual defects by looking constantly for them and by knowing what visual defects are and how to spot them. We'll talk more about visual defects a little later.

**Step #3: Judge Conformance.**

You know what the specification is and you've just measured the part. Does it conform to specification? This may seem like an easy question, but the gray areas may be a problem at times. These gray areas pop up when the measured characteristic is "*close*" to the specification, or when the specification is in itself, "*gray*".

The one thing you'll notice is that this step comes very close to step #1. It seems that when the specification is not very clear, your ability to judge whether it conforms is not very clear either. Some tips to overcome this include:

- Always quantify the specification. If it is a very subjective description, like, "*small score marks acceptable*" insist on a sample part that is an example of the "*worst case.*" This allows you to have a yardstick by which you can judge parts coming off the press.

- Use the same "*good judgement*" techniques listed in step #1.

**Step #4: Take Action.**

This step forces you to act on what you found in step #3. If the part (or characteristic) conforms to specification, you have any easy decision... run the parts.

If it does not conform, you have to make a more difficult decision... what should you do? Here are some tips to take appropriate action:

- **Double check your findings.**
	Sometimes we all misread a measuring tool.

# Inspection Steps - cont.

- **Decide on level of severity.**
	If the part is out of spec, has some major score marks or burrs, or is missing some feature or characteristics, you will need to shut off the press, inform the toolmaker, and sort the affected parts. If the problem is small and does not warrant shutting the press down, you may want to inform the toolmaker of the impending problem. Sometimes they can take preventative action to cure the problem and avoid machine downtime. Shell length, small burrs, and light score marks are examples.

- **If you shut down the press, immediately segregate the bad parts.**
	Do not allow them to get mixed into good parts. It only compounds the sorting problem.

**Over-riding Rule:** Always treat parts coming out of the press like they have leprosy. **DO NOT MIX THEM INTO THE GOOD PARTS BARREL UNTIL YOU KNOW FOR SURE THEY ARE 100% GOOD.** Your job is to anticipate problems and catch them before they get too big. By dumping bad parts into "*good parts barrels*" you turn a small problem into a large one.

**Step #5: Document Data.**

To finish up, you need to record your measurements and other information. We'll go over the paperwork you're responsible for completing a little later.

# Inspection Tools

Inspection is performed by using measuring tools and gages which compare the measured part against a known standard. The measuring tool or gage is the known standard by which you make this comparison.

Measuring tools or gages consist of two main types; variable and attribute:

- **Variable** - a variable gage measures the exact size of the feature. Variable gages are normally called measuring tools. Some of the more common tools include:
	- Calipers
	- Micrometers
	- Height indicator
	- Dial indicator

- **Attribute** - an attribute gage is a method of checking whether the feature is there or not. This is sometimes referred to as "*Go/No Go*" or "*Accept/Reject*". Examples of attribute gages include:
	- Flush pin gage (Fingernail)
	- Plug gage
	- Height gage
	- Ring gage
	- Position gage
	- Radius gage

The measuring tools and gages we'll teach you to use include the following:

- Micrometer
- Calipers
- Indicators
- Height indicator
- Ring gages
- Plug gages
- Fingernail gages
- Height gages

# Calibration

Each tool and gage is the standard by which we measure a part. To maintain the accuracy of these measuring tools we perform "calibration", which means that we compare these measuring tools to standards which have a greater accuracy than the tools themselves. This calibration is done on a frequent basis called a "calibration interval" (How often the measuring tool gets calibrated). This is the only method which assures that our standard hasn't changed.

Calibration is typically handled by the Quality Department. Ask your Supervisor or Team Leader about the system we use and what your calibration responsibilities are.

# Using a Micrometer - cont.

- [ ] Establish the line of measurement by rocking the micrometer slightly to find the maximum size. Establish the micrometer to a right angle to the axis by rocking the micrometer from side to side.

*[FIGURE: Two line drawings showing a micrometer being rocked to find the line of measurement - left: micrometer rocked across a round part to find maximum size; right: micrometer rocked side to side at a right angle to the axis of a cylindrical part.]*

- [ ] Use the clamp ring as a memory device to preserve a reading, not to turn the micrometer into a snap gage.

- [ ] Use the ratchet stop to equalize the "feel" of the micrometer. Do not use it as a guarantee. The following can cause variation in the readings when using the ratchet stop:
	- Dirt.
	- Lubrication.
	- Micrometer closed too rapidly.
	- Poor adjustment.

- [ ] Take a number of readings and mentally note your high and low readings.

- [ ] Steps for using a micrometer.
	- Adjust it to approximate size required.
	- Hold anvil against reference surface and rapidly close it until you cannot see between the spindle and the part.
	- Carefully and slowly close the spindle until the spindle touches the shell. Rock the spindle carefully back and forth and side to side. Continue closing until ratchet stop clicks once.
	- Repeat procedure several times and mentally note your high and low readings.

- [ ] Micrometers have a screw thread which is part of the spindle. Since this thread has 40 threads per inch, it has a pitch of 1/40 (.025) inch. One revolution of the spindle thus increases or decrease the distance between the measuring faces by .025.

- [ ] The faces of a micrometer can be cleaned by carefully closing them on a piece of paper, gently pulling the paper out, and softly blowing on the faces to remove particles.

- [ ] Be aware of problems that cause inaccurate measurements. These problems must be corrected before continuing.
	- Micrometer does not read zero when fully closed.
	- Measuring faces are worn from use.
	- Measuring surfaces not perpendicular to center line of micrometer.
	- Measuring faces not parallel.
	- Accurate readings in one spot and inaccurate readings in other spots along the micrometers' measuring range.

- [ ] The basic steps in calibrating the micrometer are as follows:

	- Clean the faces.

# Using a Calipers - cont.

**General Rules when using calipers:**

- Errors in using dial or digital electronic calipers:
  - Misreading the scale.
  - Worn jaws.
  - Miscalibration.
  - Damage to the calipers.
  - Using too much pressure.
  - Using too little pressure.

- Establish the line of measurement by rocking the calipers slightly to find the correct position.

- Establish the calipers at a right angle to the part by rocking it carefully side to side.

- When checking an inside surface, you must swing the jaws until they "center" on the largest internal diameter.

- If the caliper is used with too much measurement pressure, the entire caliper will bow or spring.

- To calibrate the accuracy of a caliper:
  - Check jaws for wear by closing them tightly and looking through the crack towards a light. No light should be seen.
  - Make repeated checks of known standards at different sizes to test accuracy.
  - Set the dial to zero after repeated checks of known standards.

- Steps for using a calipers:
  - Assess whether this is the best device available for this measurement.
  - Clean both the part to be measured and the caliper.
  - Loosen the lock screw and set the movable jaw slightly larger than the dimension to be measured.
  - Place the fixed jaw against the reference point of the part.
  - Align the beam of the caliper as parallel to the line of measurement as possible - in both planes.
  - Turn the fine adjustment roll so that the movable jaw just touches the part. Rock the calipers slightly to obtain correct seat of jaws and feel.
  - Read in place, without disturbing calipers.
  - Repeat this procedure a number of times and note the high and low readings.
  - Clean and lubricate calipers.

# Using Indicators

An indicator is a tool used to compare sizes and measurements to a known standard. An indicator can be of the dial or electronic digital type.

If you're already familiar with how to use an indicator, you may read the General Rules below. If you've never used an indicator, go to your Team Leader and ask them to assist you in learning how to read an indicator. When you're comfortable with the basics of reading and using it, proceed with the rest of this section.

*[FIGURE: Two labeled diagrams side by side. LEFT "DIAL INDICATOR": parts labeled HAND, BEZEL CLAMP, STEM CAP, BEZEL, CASE, DIAL, STEM, CRYSTAL, CONTACT POINT. RIGHT "DIAL TEST INDICATOR": parts labeled UNIVERSAL FRICTION HOLDER, DIAL, HAND, CONTACT POINT.]*

**General Rules when using indicators:**

- Most indicators work on a rack and pinion principle. Any movement of the spindle is magnified and transmitted to a hand pointer over a graduated dial or digitally. The contact point is held in constant contact with the work surface by a spring.

- Dial indicators are available in two types:
  - Continuous reading which allows you to measure long distances - they can be of the regular range (2 ½ revolutions of travel) or long-range (a second small hand indicates number of revolutions large hand has taken).
  - Dial test indicators which have a balanced dial - one that reads to both the left and right and indicates a plus or minus value.
  - Dial test indicators have a balanced dial - one that reads to both the left and right and indicates a plus or minus value. Dial test indicators can be set at any position through a 180° arc.

- Dial test indicators have two types of dials:
  - Balanced - can read in both a plus or a minus distance from zero.
  - Continuous - can read in one direction only.

- Some indicators have a revolution counter which counts how many revolutions the indicator needle has taken.

# Using Indicators - cont.

- Indicators come in a variety of contact tips. Here are some general rules for choosing a correct tip:
  - Round work - flat or rounded tip
  - Flat work - rounded tip
  - Make sure contact tip is placed on high spot of work surface.

- Dial indicators need special care. They are a very delicate instrument and must be treated as such. Here are some tips for their use:
  - Never bump, throw, or drop them.
  - Keep free of dirt and grit.
  - Store them in a safe, dry case in a place where they won't get banged around.
  - Do not use air pressure to clean them.
  - Do not hammer on a workpiece while the indicator is in contact.

- Errors in using dial indicators:
  - Incorrect travel - you must remember how many revolutions your pointer has taken.
  - Insufficient travel - the pointer should engage about ½ revolution from the dead position.
  - Parallax - you must look straight at the dial when you read the indicator. If you look from the side, the reading could be incorrect.
  - Cosine error - when the tip is at an angle to the surface being measured. The tip as it moves, must move either in a parallel or a perpendicular direction, compared with the workpiece. Cosine error is illustrated below:
