# Introduction To Safe Work Practices

Safety is the top priority in our company. It is so important that it ranks higher than quality, delivery, and cost issues.

There is always the potential for injury in our plant. If you look around you'll see conditions, situations, behaviors, and attitudes which could lead to an accident. The prevention of these accidents is the subject of this section. Each person in the company must take personal responsibility to develop and improve their own safe work practices. We can reach our safety goal of *"zero accidents"* only through the safe practices and prevention techniques of each person.

So how do you develop and improve your own safe work practices? We've found some guidelines that will assist you in developing your own safe practices. These guidelines are organized around four distinct categories: attitudes, behaviors, conditions, and situations.

### Attitudes

Our safety goal is *"zero accidents"*. To reach this goal every one of us must believe that all near misses, accidents, and injuries can and must be prevented. If you begin with that *"attitude"*, you've sensitized your mind to seek out and eliminate potential hazards.

Many of the people with excellent safety records are *"paranoid"* about safety. They understand the dangers and are constantly aware of what they're doing and how they're doing it. They have an excellent safety record because they take personal responsibility for their own safety and the safety of others.

On the other hand, show me a person who says *"Hey, the workplace is full of dangers! Accidents are bound to happen!"* and I'd be willing to bet that that person, department, or company has a poor safety record.

Safety begins with YOU. Do you believe that all near-misses, accidents, and injuries can be prevented? If you don't, you've got some work to do! Our company will not accept anything but your best when it comes to working safely!

# Introduction To Safe Work Practices - cont.

### Behaviors

Safe work practices begin with attitude... a belief that all near-misses, accidents, and injuries can be prevented. The only way someone can *"measure"* your attitude is to observe your behavior... the things you do every day.

The following list will help you recognize if your daily behavior is a reflection of a safety conscious attitude and safe work practices.

A person who is conscious of safety:

- [ ] Believes that all near misses, accidents, and injuries can be prevented.
- [ ] Adheres to all safety policies, rules and guidelines.
- [ ] Informs and instructs others about safety policies, rules, and guidelines.
- [ ] Confronts other people who are working in an unsafe manner.
- [ ] Takes personal responsibility for safety—both their own and those around them.
- [ ] Consistently demonstrates safe working practices on the job.
- [ ] Informs the appropriate person or corrects unsafe conditions or situations.
- [ ] Analyzes the job for ways to work safer.

A person who ignores safety issues or is not conscious of safety:

- [ ] Believes that accidents are just that—they just happen because of the dangers on the job.
- [ ] Adheres to all safety policies, rules, and guidelines when convenient or given no choice.
- [ ] Doesn't worry about how other people view safety.
- [ ] Ignores other people who are working in an unsafe manner.
- [ ] Believes that safety is the Supervisors job.
- [ ] Will take unsafe chances if necessary.
- [ ] Believes that unsafe conditions or situations are someone else's problem unless it directly affects them.
- [ ] Doesn't really think about safety when doing the job.

As you can see, a safety conscious person is easy to spot by their talk, their attitude, and their actions. If you had even one "✓" in the right hand column you need to analyze your own view of safety. Discussing this with co-workers and/or your Supervisor may help you uncover areas of safety improvement within yourself and others. Take the time to find out how you can help and take action on the suggestions given.

# Introduction To Safe Work Practices - cont.

### Conditions

Working safely also means that the conditions you work in must be safe. This means that potential hazards are identified and either eliminated or placed out of reach. It's the reason presses have guards around them. It's the reason oil spills on the floor are mopped up and then the cause of the spill or leak is found and fixed.

There are hundreds of potential *"conditions"* around you all the time. Your job here is two-fold. You must:

- [ ] Adhere to all safety policies, rules, and guidelines regarding press guarding, operation of presses and equipment, clean up etc. You must know and follow all of these practices consistently.
- [ ] Identify and take appropriate action to report and/or correct those conditions which create unsafe conditions.

A simple, but effective strategy for taking appropriate action on an unsafe condition is to:

1. Take immediate action to shut off power, remove the person from the area, etc. to prevent an accident from occurring where there is immediate danger.
2. Immediately contact your Supervisor.
3. Assess the situation.
4. Develop action plans to solve the problem.

Always be aware of the conditions around you and immediately contact your Supervisor if you feel they are unsafe.

### Situations

Your job places you in contact with many potentially unsafe situations every day. It's your response to them that makes all the difference. These are different from *"conditions"* in that they are created by the occurrence of perhaps two or more events at one time. Here are some examples:

- [ ] You walk around a corner into an oncoming Hi-lo...
- [ ] As you're working behind a press, someone turns the press over thinking it had stopped on it's own...
- [ ] As you drive the Hi-lo into the nose of a trailer, the trailer tips forward...
- [ ] When you walk up to a surface grinder you run your hand over the magnet to clean it. However, the last person forgot to turn off the wheel...
- [ ] As you walk by a stock reel, a coil of steel snaps into you after the operator cuts the banding...

Sound familiar? These are examples of situations that could occur if only one person forgets a safety rule just one time! Think about the problems if all of us would forget once in a while!

So what can be done? Every person must adhere to safe work practices all the time... every time! Also, you must work *"defensively"* ... this means to always pay attention to what's going on around you. That's the way we'll reach our safety goal of *"zero accidents"*. Begin today by taking personal responsibility for your own safety attitude and actions.

# General Safety Rules

### General Safety Rules

There are many safety rules that apply to the work you do every day. Some of the general rules are listed below. Remember that each piece of equipment or tool may have it's own safety rules. You are responsible to know what these are too, and to follow them. They may or may not be included in this general list. Also, your Supervisor may post other safety procedures in your area.

- Report all near misses, accidents, and injuries (no matter how slight) to your Supervisor immediately, as well as anything that needs repair or is a safety hazard.
- Avoid overloading electrical outlets with too many appliances or machines.
- Use flammable items, such as cleaning fluids, with caution.
- Walk — don't run.
- Use stairs one at a time.
- Report to your Supervisor if you or a co-worker becomes ill or is injured.
- Ask for assistance when lifting heavy objects.
- Smoke only in designated smoking areas.
- Keep cabinet doors and file and desk drawers closed when not in use.
- Never empty an ash tray into a waste basket or open receptacle.
- Sit firmly and squarely in chairs that roll or tilt.
- Wear or use appropriate safety equipment as required in your work.
- Avoid *"horseplay"* or practical jokes.
- Start work on any machine only after safety procedures and requirements have been explained (and you understand them).
- Use air hoses only for the use intended. Do not blow air at yourself or anyone else.
- Wear appropriate personal protective equipment, like shoes, hats, gloves, goggles, spats, hearing protectors, etc., in designated areas or when working on an operation which requires their use.
- Keep your work area clean and orderly, and the aisles clear.
- Stack materials only to safe heights.
- Watch out for the safety of fellow employees.
- Use the right tool for the job, and use it correctly.
- Wear gloves whenever handling weldments, metal, scrap, barrels, etc.
- Operate Hi-los and motorized equipment only if trained and certified by the company.
- Always use and maintain any press guarding and safety devices.

Remember, failure to adhere to these rules will be considered serious infractions of safety rules and will result in disciplinary actions.

# Safety Specifics: Cuts and Sprains

Of all the possible accidents to write about, you may wonder why we've chosen *"cuts"* and *"sprains"*. The reason for it is that in most stamping plants like ours, cuts and sprains account for approximately 80% or more of all accidents, with cuts making up the largest single accident cause.

Because of this fact, we'll be looking at cuts and sprains in this section. This doesn't mean that you can ignore other accident types. In fact, you must ask your Supervisor if there are other specific safety training courses required for your job. This course cannot, and does not, cover all safety training and education required for your job.

### Cuts

Cuts to the hand are the largest single accident cause in metal stamping plants like ours. Cuts can be caused by:

- [ ] Falling objects
- [ ] Raw material
- [ ] Scrap material
- [ ] Sharp objects or tools
- [ ] Pinching, shearing, or squeezing tools
- [ ] Wheels, rollers, and gears
- [ ] Scrap choppers
- [ ] Etc.

Prevention of these injuries begins by recognizing the hazards, following all safety rules, and practicing the following safe work habits:

- [ ] **Never place your hands or any part of your body within the die area of the press.**
- [ ] Never operate, service, adjust the press, or install dies without proper instructions and without full authorization. If you have not been trained for the job to which you are assigned, see your Supervisor.
- [ ] **Never attempt to remove a workpiece or scrap from the die with your hands. If any material sticks in the die, stop the press and call your Supervisor.**
- [ ] **Fingers are precious! Keep all of them by keeping them clear of danger areas and pinch points.**
- [ ] Check the condition and operation of point of operation safeguarding. Be sure that all safeguards are in place and functioning correctly. If you don't know, please ask your Supervisor.
- [ ] Keep all point of operation safeguarding in place and in good condition.
- [ ] **Never** reach over, under, or around press guards and never try to beat, cheat, or modify any point of operation safeguarding.
- [ ] Never stand or sit on anything while operating a press that could cause you to fall, slip, or stumble into the press or any auxiliary equipment.
- [ ] Clean the press daily and inspect carefully for loose, worn, broken, or damaged parts. Also keep the area around the press clean and orderly.
- [ ] Improper material handling methods cause accidents. Plan ahead and arrange a safe method of transferring materials. Use mechanical handling methods (instead of manual) whenever possible.
- [ ] All scrap material should be placed in the containers provided. Never allow pieces of scrap to project over the sides of the container.
- [ ] Follow all safety rules and regulations for your area and job. Report all unsafe conditions to your Supervisor immediately!

### Sprains

While sprains do not account for the large volume of accidents that cuts do, sprains are a serious accident type. Back sprains typically are the second leading accident type in plants like ours.

As with all safety issues, prevention is your best insurance against back injury. Many safety experts recommend:

- [ ] Keeping your back in mind by following proper lifting and material handling techniques.
- [ ] Always think ahead by lifting everything twice — once mentally in your mind and only then physically.

### Sprains - cont.

*Material Handling Tips*

- [ ] Size up the load.
  How much does it weigh? How much can you safely lift yourself? What size, or how bulky is it? Do not attempt too much on your own.
- [ ] Get help.
  Don't get a superman's complex and think that you can't ask for help. Always ask for help if something is too bulky or heavy for one person to handle adequately.
- [ ] Find a better way.
  Many times you can find a different way like using a crane, Hi-lo, cart, hand truck, or other device.
- [ ] Push, don't pull.
  If you must move something heavy on wheels, always push the load, don't pull it.
- [ ] Check and clear the pathway.
  Remove obstacles and plan the best route.
- [ ] Remove repetitive lifting if possible.
  Try to find alternative methods to lifting.
- [ ] Don't twist when you carry.
  Twisting puts incredible strain on the back.
- [ ] Watch your footing when carrying.
  Wear the right shoes and watch for and clear trip and slip hazards.
- [ ] Split one big load into smaller loads.
- [ ] Decide how you'll lift and carry before you pick it up.

Using these tips for every load could save you a back injury.

# How Do You View Housekeeping?

Housekeeping is a job that never ends. Many people see it as a monotonous and boring activity. They either ignore it, hoping it will go away, or they do it only when someone gets on their case. It's probably the primary reason for our plant looking dirty and disorganized at times. Another reason is that we get busy. We get so busy, in fact, that we forget the importance of good housekeeping practices.

# Why is Housekeeping Important?

Let's suppose that you are choosing a day-care facility for your kids. You go and visit each one with a list of questions. You want to see for yourself which is the best for your children. You walk into the first one and you see that there are toys laying all over. They're stuffed into corners, under furniture, and it's difficult to move around. You walk around a little. You find spilled food, milk, and other not so nice messes. What's your impression? You'd probably walk out the door, right?

Now put yourself back on the job. Look around you. What is the first thing our customers and suppliers notice when they walk into the plant? Just like the day-care example, it's probably how clean and organized the plant is. Will they turn around and walk out based just on a first impression of a messy plant? Probably not, but it has made an impression.

Our goal is to make our plant a place that is continuously clean and organized. We want to create a first impression that says, *"We're proud of the way our plant looks. You can be sure that we produce parts with the same attention to detail and quality."* Reaching this goal can take place only through committed and dedicated people. It is your responsibility, along with every other associate, to maintain this level of excellence. I challenge you to take pride in our environment. I challenge you to replace drudgery and boredom with pride. Let's do it together.

# Housekeeping Practices

One thing we've found, and perhaps you have too, is that much of the drudgery can be taken out of housekeeping by simply preventing messes in the first place. Instead of ignoring it until it builds up to a huge job, do a little at a time. Here are some ideas to help you do this:

- [ ] Dump paper and scrap immediately, rather than letting it accumulate.
  - There are containers used for recycling paper. Use these for paper only, not candy wrappers, plastic, etc.
  - There are trash containers for all other paper scrap and garbage located in each press area.
  - There are scrap containers for metal scrap. Do not mix different types of metal scrap together. Do not dump trash into metal scrap containers.
- [ ] Pick up pieces of paper, scrap, and other garbage as you walk past them. Don't let them lay.
- [ ] Mop up spills and oily spots immediately.
- [ ] Carry a rag in your pocket so that you can wipe off fingerprints and oil spots as they occur. Better yet, use the rag to open cabinets and press guards if you have dirty hands. This prevents fingerprints - which means you never have to go back and wipe them off.
- [ ] Return brooms, dustpans, mops, mop buckets, tools, and equipment to its designated spot immediately after using. You'll also be able to find it the next time.
- [ ] Look for and correct things that create a mess. Things like:
  - Fix a leaky scrap chute.
  - Ask for an empty barrel for an area that does not have a waste receptacle.
  - Designate areas for items for things that don't have a home.
  - Ask for a funnel rather than pouring without one.

  If you see a problem, see your Supervisor if it is something you cannot easily fix yourself.
- [ ] Clean and return tools and equipment immediately after you use them.
- [ ] Dump paper, bands, and other clutter in their proper container immediately during or after a coil change.
- [ ] Dispose of dirty rags in the appropriate container.
- [ ] Keep benches organized and free of clutter.
- [ ] Keep floors clean and free of clutter.
- [ ] Wear gloves when dumping scrap or handling anything with sharp edges or with the capability of cutting your hands.
- [ ] Use clean containers for steel scrap to avoid contamination. This is especially true for copper and/or brass scrap.

# Housekeeping Tools and Equipment

There are several pieces of tools and equipment used for housekeeping. They include:

### Rags

**Where found:**
1. Ask your Supervisor where clean rags can be found.
2. Ask your Supervisor where dirty rags can be disposed of.

**How used:** Rags are used to clean hands, benches, etc. They cut down the number of trips needed to wash your hands. They are also used to clean up small spills.

**Hints:**
1. The rags we use get cleaned by an outside service. Please use the rag until it is dirty, since discarding it early only increases the number of rags we use. This can get very expensive. Don't use one so long, though, that it begins to make things dirty all by itself!
2. When cleaning a dirty press, a spill, or something very oily, use some of the already dirty rags to clean up a majority of the oil. This saves rags.

### Brooms

**Where found:** Ask your Supervisor where brooms can be found.

**How used:** Brooms can be of the straight or push variety. Use the broom best suited to the job you have to do. Return after use.

**Hints:**
1. Clean metal chips and shavings from the brushes periodically for best results.
2. After sweeping, dump all scrap, paper, shavings, and parts into the trash can. Do not dump anything found on the floor into a metal scrap container. This will contaminate the steel.

### Dustpans

**Where found:** Ask your Supervisor where dustpans can be found.

**Hints:**
1. Clean with rag after use so that it doesn't become coated with grease and oil.
