# Cup Station Components

A cup station takes the blank and forms it into a shell of a specific diameter and height. The cup is formed using the following components:

1. Chuck or punch holder
2. Punch
3. Pressure pad (in a Baird, the blank punch becomes the pressure pad)
4. Cup die
5. Knockout
6. Pressure pad linkage
   a. Pressure pad/arm
   b. Lower arm
   c. Linkage adjustment screw

Take a moment to find each of these components on the cross-section.

# Cup Station Components - cont.

The components for a cup station are narrowed to just the ICOP/Platarg press for this module. The reason for this is because the Baird cup tools were discussed earlier in the "mount the blank" module.

The pressure pad linkage deserves special attention in our discussion of cup station components. Two adjustments must be made with this unit:

- Pressure on the blank
- Stripper height adjustment

*[FIGURE: Diagram of the pressure pad linkage, labeled (top to bottom): top linkage arm, linkage screw lock nut, linkage screw, stop bracket, stripping height collar, stripping height collar lock nuts, linkage screw lock nut, lower linkage arm.]*

### Pressure adjustment

The pressure adjustment is made using the threaded rod which joins the pressure pad arm and the lower arm.

- Increasing the distance between these arms backs off the pressure on the blank.
- Decreasing the distance between them increases the pressure on the blank.

The threaded adjustment rod has a right-handed thread on one end and a left-handed thread on the other. When this adjustment rod is turned, the distance between the arms either becomes longer or shorter.

### Stripper height adjustment

The stripper height adjustment is made by changing the height at which the pressure pad arms come to rest in the fully up position. This adjustment has no effect on the pressure adjustment.

This adjustment is made by moving a collar up and down on the adjustment rod. Moving the collar down the adjustment rod sets the pressure pad arms to come up higher, and moving it up the adjustment rod sets the pressure pad arms lower.

# Mounting and Adjusting Cup Tools

The procedures for mounting and adjusting cup tools are as follows:

1. **Remove burrs** — check for and remove burrs from tooling and press.
2. **Clean tools and plunger** — wipe tooling and plunger and clean of dirt and foreign material.
3. **Insert dogs** — insert dogs (with smaller diameter and shorter length shanks than standard) into the pressure pad arm. These dogs are a different size because a standard dog would stick out of the pressure pad arm, bottoming out or rubbing on the blank die retainer.
4. **Insert pressure pad** — insert pressure pad into pressure pad arm opening and onto dogs. The easiest way to do this is to pull up the pressure pad arm and insert the pressure pad from the bottom.
5. **Mount cup punch** — insert punch into pressure pad, thread punch into plunger, and tighten securely.
6. **Back off pressure and stripper adjustments** — back off the stripper adjustment so that the stripper height is taller than the cup slide sample or the fingers, whichever is taller. Back off the pressure adjustment so that the space between the pressure pad and the cup die is more than material thickness.
7. **Check movement of pressure pad** — jog slide to the forward position. Push down on the pressure pad arm to determine if pressure pad moves freely into transfer block. The pressure pad arm must clear the blank die retainer and the pressure pad must freely enter the transfer block and stop on top of the cup die.
8. **Jog punch into die** — jog punch down to about 1/4 inch above die. Loosen front die bolt and pry punch into die. Jog punch to bottom of stroke. Snug front die bolt.
9. **Adjust pressure** — insert a strip of raw material or feeler gage of the same thickness between the pressure pad and the cup die. Turn the adjustment screw to move the pressure pad downward until the feeler gage becomes snug. Tighten both lock nuts securely. Jog punch to the first lift position.
10. **Adjust cup punch timing** — loosen the lifter bar and adjust the punch so that it is two to three material thicknesses below the die surface.
11. **Adjust knockout cam** — loosen the knockout cam and adjust it so that the knockout is two to three material thicknesses below the die surface. Tighten the knockout cam bolts securely.
12. **Adjust stripper height** — jog the press to the stripper delay position. Loosen the stop collar lock nut on the adjustment rod. Adjust the collar to correctly set the stripper height, using a slide sample from this station. Tighten the stop collar lock nut securely.

   Note: With some jobs, the wall will be shorter than the fingers. In these cases, you must adjust the pressure pad to clear the fingers in its stripping position.

13. **Make a cup** — jog press to top of stroke, with slide in forward position, and a blank in the transfer block. Loosen up the cup die block and jog the punch into the die. Tighten up the die block securely. Jog the punch back up to top and make another shell. Jog the press very slowly while checking to make sure all components are working properly.

14. **Inspect cup** — inspect the shell for length, concentricity of radii and flange, scratches, appearance, and make adjustments to match it to the slide sample. Adjust pressure to remove wrinkles, but not to exert more pressure than is needed to remove wrinkles (wall thickness of cup must match or improve upon that of the slide sample).

    Note: **Shimming the punch**

    In some cases the fingers will not deliver the shell directly on center even though the slide is adjusted correctly. Because of this, the shell will have an irregular flange, radii, and draw rings, even though the punch and die are aligned properly. To alleviate this condition, the punch must be shimmed so that it becomes centered within the shell, after which the die is realigned to the new center. The following procedure can be used after you determine that the punch must be shimmed:

    1. Bar or jog the punch downward until it enters the shell.
    2. Loosen the chuck and place the proper thickness shim behind the chuck in a location which, after tightening the chuck securely, will center the punch within the shell.
    3. Loosen the die and jog the shell down into it for proper alignment. Tighten the die bolts. (Care must be taken when a large shim is used because the punch and shell may no longer enter the die properly.)
    4. Repeat this process until the shell has uniform flange, radii, and draw rings.

15. **Check operation** — shell must be gripped firmly by the fingers and transferred to the next station without interruption of operation.

16. **Double-check all adjustments** — double-check all adjustments and the tightness of all bolts.

# Correct Cup Station Problems

Cup stations can develop a number of problems. The list below gives you some common problems, a few causes for each one, and the possible solutions for the causes:

| Problem | Possible Causes | Solutions |
|---------|-----------------|-----------|
| 1. Wrinkles in the flange | a. Not enough pressure | a. Increase pressure exerted on blank by pressure pad |
| | b. Blank cut-out | b. See module on troubleshooting blank |
| | c. Pressure pad surface is rough | c. Re-grind and lap surface of pressure pad |
| | d. Roller(s) in pressure pad lower arm are bad | d. Replace rollers and bearings |
| | e. Foreign material in cup station | e. Clean thoroughly; find cause and solve |
| | f. Too much clearance between punch and die | f. Check material thickness; check tooling sizes |
| | g. Broken stripper dog | g. Replace |
| | h. Pressure pad ground at taper | h. Regrind |
| 2. Shell radii larger on one side than the other | a. Misaligned punch and die | a. Realign |
| | b. Non-concentric radii on tooling | b. Check and re-grind or replace |
| | c. Fingers misaligned with tooling | c. Shim punch and realign die |
| 3. Bulge in side wall | a. Timing/adjustment of components | a. Knockout timed of shell too fast; punch timed too slow; stripper set too low |

| | c. Shavings and/or chips | c. See blank troubleshooting section; check for sharp edge at back side of carbide on cup die; make sure flange is protected by stripper during operation |
