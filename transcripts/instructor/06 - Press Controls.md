# Press Controls

As a Press Operator you are the first line of defense in preventing problems at the press. The reason for this is because you spend most of your time on the floor by the press. This puts you in a unique role. Your knowledge, skills, and even your eyes and ears must be used to prevent problems from occurring. If we look back at all the knowledge and skills we've covered so far we can't help but notice that almost all of them are geared to anticipating and/or preventing problems. Your job is crucial to the success of the company. One missed problem can cost thousands of dollars in scrap parts or equipment repair.

In this section we complete your current training by concentrating on the press itself. We'll cover press controls, press start up, and common production problems.

# Production Problems

Some people refer to a press that is running as "*a problem waiting to happen*". The biggest question is, "*what problem will pop up and cause trouble?*" Since you don't know the answer to this question you must be prepared for almost anything.

Some people never really think about this, and as a result, they just wait until problems happen. They then get someone to fix it and wait for the next problem. This is a very reactionary approach.

In our system you are viewed as a member of our team. The success of our entire team is based, in part, on how well we can anticipate problems and eliminate them from ever happening. This is called prevention. The main ingredient in preventing problems is to be prepared. This means that you must know what could go wrong, rather than simply detecting it once it occurs.

You've probably realized by now that everything you've learned so far will help you prevent problems. This last list of problems ties each of the modules together by giving you a list of things to look for each time you walk up to a press. It's one more way of preparing yourself to prevent problems.

Each time you approach a press you must check for the following:

- [ ] 1. Is the press area neat, clean and organized?
- [ ] 2. Does the lubrication system reservoir have enough oil in it and is the oil pressure normal?
- [ ] 3. Is there enough coolant in the reservoir?
- [ ] 4. Is the coolant flowing into the tooling in the same location and with the same velocity as before?
- [ ] 5. Does the stock have any visual problems?
- [ ] 6. Is the scrap chopper operating correctly?
- [ ] 7. Are there parts and/or scrap coming out of each chute? Are they staying in the tubs? Are the tubs full?
- [ ] 8. Are the parts visually correct? Are there missing holes, score marks, or smashed parts?
- [ ] 9. Are the guard doors all closed and does everything look the same as it did?
- [ ] 10. Can you detect any noises that were not present before?

# Production Problems - cont.

Being constantly aware of these things can save lot's of problems. You've learned most of these and are doing them on your job. This list will help condense what you've learned so that you're prepared to spot problems before they become critical. If one of these questions points out a future problem, you must deal with it immediately. The urgency of the problem will tell you how fast to respond. For example, a coolant tank that needs to be filled up may not warrant the quick action (shutting off the press) that you would take if the coolant was no longer flowing into the tooling.

Your goal is to "*run quality parts consistently over a period of time*". There are two things to keep in mind while doing this:

1. *The parts must be good* - running bad parts only builds costs. It would cost you less if the press is shut off.

2. *The press must be running* - in order to run parts the press must be running. Always plan your activities around times when the press will be down anyway. For example, if the Bijur reservoir must be filled once per day, you should not be shutting the press down in the middle of the day to do it. Do it at shift end, trim sharpening, or some other time when the press is shut down anyway. Again, the press must run to make parts. However, don't run it if there is something wrong with the press itself that will cause damage, just for the sake of running parts. If in doubt, always ask your supervisor. Preventing problems by doing the job tasks you've learned will help keep the press running consistently over a long period of time. This will have a dramatic impact on the bottom line.

# Press Start Up

One of the things you must do as an operator is to start up the press in one or more of the following situations:

- After coil changes.
- At the beginning of the shift.
- When you've shut down the press for routine activities.
- In other situations, after approval from toolmaker or supervisor.

We've left this task of turning over a press until last because it can get you in the most trouble in the biggest hurry.

**Turning over a press**

The first thing you must learn about starting up a press is how to "*turn it over*". "*Turning a press over*" means to run the press through one stroke or revolution. When turning a press over, you must consider the costs of making a mistake. Breaking a transfer finger or piece of tooling could cost many thousands of dollars when you factor in building the part, fitting it up, and the machine downtime costs while all that is happening. You must realize when you turn a press over that any mistake could cause thousands of dollars of damage and downtime, as well as damage a customer relationship.

So does this mean you must live in fear of turning a press over? Well, yes and no. After this module you should not be afraid of turning a press over, but you must always be paranoid about what could happen when you do. So, how do experienced operators become comfortable with turning a press over? They prepare themselves by learning everything they must know and do before turning the press over. They know that a press can be turned over only after they've checked each of the following:

- [ ] Does the tooling and the parts in the slide have adequate lubrication? Is the coolant running into the tooling in the same location and with the same velocity as before?
- [ ] Are all the parts in the fingers? A part missing out of the slide is a big red flag. Do not turn over a press with a part missing. Remember, that part must be somewhere and if you don't see it you could cause serous damage if it's still in there, in the wrong place. Pay special attention to two areas:
  - If you don't see a blank don't turn the press over. The material must be going somewhere. Turning the press over with two blanks could blow the entire blank station, or more.
  - Always check the entire slide, every time!
  - Always know where each shell went to - account for each one!
- [ ] Visually check each station. But, don't focus on one spot. I've seen people so intent on the blank station that they forget the rest of the press. Always do a visual for the following:
  - Parts in fingers.
  - Scrap in slide.
  - Tooling tight and aligned.
  - Any abnormal situations.

# Press Start Up - cont.

- [ ] Follow good safety practices like the following:
  - Close all guards and covers.
  - Be aware of the entire press area.
  - Do not turn the press over if any safety device is unhooked, removed, or inoperable.
  - Do not turn over the press if any part of your body or someone else's body is inside the press enclosure.
  - All other shop safety practices.

**While turning over the press, you must do the following:**

- [ ] Turn the press over one revolution. Always stop the press on the top of the stroke.
- [ ] Do your visuals between each revolution as you're turning the press over.
- [ ] Do not turn the press over more times than is necessary. It only invites trouble because the probe system does not automatically shut the press down if their is a problem when you have it in the inch or cycle mode.
- [ ] Look at the probe lights after each revolution to make sure they are working properly. Never use this as a visual to see if the parts are all there, though.
- [ ] If a part is missing, you've found it (or know where it went), and you must jog this empty spot down to the end, always check with your supervisor or toolmaker. There are some jobs that have stations that must have a shell in them. This doesn't happen very often, but you should check for each job just to make sure.

# Control Panel

To turn the press over and eventually start it up requires that you know the press control panel. Because each panel is slightly different, we're going to cover the basic controls that you'll find on an eyelet press. If you must turn a non-eyelet press over make sure you received instruction first. However, much of the information below will be used on other presses as well.

| Control | Description |
|---|---|
| "*Emergency Stop*" | this is the big red button that shuts everything down when you push it. If you need to stop the press immediately because of a problem, this is the button to push. It is not a good practice to stop the press with this button unless it is an emergency. |
| "*Mode*" switch | this is a three position switch which lets you select "*OFF*", "*Inch*", or "*continuous*".<br>• the "*off*" position is used when you are not using inch or continuous and for times when using other features.<br>• the "*inch*" position is used when you must jog the press or turn it over before startup. This is sometimes labeled the "*Jog*" position.<br>• the "*continuous*" position is used when you are running the press automatically. |
| "*Setup*" button | this button must be pressed before the press will run automatically. |
| "*Run*" buttons | these are two buttons that must be pressed simultaneously to jog or start up the press. When starting the press, they must be engaged for several seconds or through ½ of a revolution of the press. |
| "*Motor start*" button | this button starts the motor which turns the flywheel. The motor must be "*on*" to jog or run the press. |
| "*Motor stop*" button | this button stops the flywheel. It is not a good practice to stop the motor while the press is running. |
| "*Inch*" switch<br>"*Inch*" button | these buttons are found on some presses to inch the press instead of using the two hand control. You must hold the switch to the inch position while pushing the inch button to jog the press. |
| "*Stop on top*" button | this button should be used to stop the press in normal situations. It automatically stops the press at the top of the stroke after being pushed. |
| "*Coolant*" switch | this switch controls the coolant pump and can be switched "*on*" or "*off*". |

Again, some presses may have additional buttons and lights. These are the ones you'll need to turn over, or start a press. If you must turn over or start a press that you are unfamiliar with, always ask your supervisor or a toolmaker to demonstrate and explain the panel to you.

# Probe System

The probe system of a press monitors each shell as it travels through the press. Each shell is detected by a probe wire which sends a signal to the control panel. A light goes on when the probe wire detects the shell in the transfer finger. If every probe has detected a shell the press will continue to run automatically. If a probe fails to detect a shell it will automatically shut off the press. The light corresponding to that probe will be off, showing which probe has missed a part.

Under normal conditions you will not have to operate the probe system. You should never adjust probes, switch wires, or change settings in any way. Occasionally the reset light will come on signaling that something other than a part has touched the probe. Never just flip the reset switch to reset the unit and restart the press. Always find out why the reset light came on. Ask your Team Leader what your responsibilities are when the probe system has shut down the press and how to operate the system if it is your responsibility to do so.

# Press Control Operation

**WARNING:**

NEVER OPERATE A PRESS WITHOUT INSTRUCTION AND SUBSEQUENT PRACTICE. YOU MUST BE CERTIFIED BEFORE OPERATING ANY EQUIPMENT. THESE GUIDELINES AND STEPS ARE MEANT TO BE GENERAL AND ARE NOT A SUBSTITUTE FOR SPECIFIC TRAINING ON THE EQUIPMENT YOU MUST OPERATE.

The basic tasks for operating press controls that you must become skilled at, include:

1. Turning the press over - barring
2. Turning the press over - also called inching or jogging.
3. Starting up the press.
4. Stopping the press.
5. Emergency stop.

Assuming that the press was running previously, the steps for doing each are as follows:

**Turning over the press - barring**

Step 1: Perform all visual checks and safety activities.
Step 2: Push the "*Motor Stop*" or "*Emergency*" button to stop the motor and flywheel.
Step 3: Turn the "*Mode*" switch to "*Bar*"
Step 4: Place a bar in the flywheel or press the foot pedal while pushing the "*Bar*" button in short quick taps.
Step 5: Always perform all visual checks and safety activities while "*Barring*" and before continuing.

**Turning over the press - inching or jogging**

Step 1: Perform all visual checks and safety activities.
Step 2: Push the "*Motor start*" button to start the motor and flywheel.
Step 3: Turn the "*coolant*" switch to "*on*" to start the coolant.
Step 4: Turn the "*mode*" switch to "*inch*".
Step 5: Push both "*run*" buttons simultaneously

**OR**

Turn the "*inch*" switch while pushing the "*inch*" button to jog the press.
Step 6: Always perform all visual checks and safety activities before continuing.

# Press Control Operation - cont.

**Starting the press - continuous run.**

**Note:** It is good practice to turn the press over through one revolution to make sure everything is operating properly, before running continuous.

Step 1: Perform all visual checks and safety activities.
Step 2: Push the "*motor start*" button to start the motor and flywheel.
Step 3: Turn the "*coolant*" switch to "*on*" to start the coolant.
Step 4: Turn the "*mode*" switch to "*continuous*".
Step 5: Press the "*setup*" button.
Step 6: Push both "*run*" buttons simultaneously and hold them through ½ revolution of the press.

**Stopping the press**

Step 1: Push the "*stop on top*" button to stop the press.
Step 2: Push "*motor stop*" button to stop the motor and flywheel.

**Emergency stop**

Step 1: Push the "*emergency stop*" button.
