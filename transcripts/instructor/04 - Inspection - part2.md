# Using Attribute Gages

Attribute gages are gages that tell you if a dimension is within blueprint limits or not. They do not give you an actual dimension. The most common types of attribute gages are plug, ring, fingernail, height gages, and radius gages.

If you're already familiar with how to use attribute gages, you may read the General Rules below. If you've never used attribute gages, go to your Team Leader and ask them to assist you in learning how to read attribute gages. When you're comfortable with the basics of reading and using them, proceed with the rest of this section.

- [ ] **Plug** - hardened steel pins that fit in a handle. There is usually a go and a no/go side. They are used to check inside diameters on an accept or "reject" basis. There are three types of plug gages.
  - Single end - a go or a no/go end only.
  - Double end - both a go and a no/go end.
  - Progressive - where the go and no/go are on the same gage, with the go portion first.

- [ ] **Ring** - hardened steel rings that measure outside diameters. They can come as two separate rings (one go and the other no/go) or as a single progressive ring with two diameters in it. The no/go ring has a groove on the outside diameter to identify it as the no/go ring.

- [ ] **Flush pin** - they not only tell you if the dimension is in or out of blueprint specification, but also approximately where within the tolerance it is. The tolerance step can be either on the bar or the pin.

- [ ] **Height** - these measure whether or not a part is the right height. They can only tell you if it is too tall or too short. They cannot give you an actual size.

- [ ] **Radius gages** - these measure a radius by comparing the radius to a standard.

### General rules when using attribute gages:

- [ ] Attribute gages will not detect the following:
  - Out of round shaft.
  - Tapered shaft.
  - Hour-glass shaped hole.
  - Bell-mouthed hole.
  - Tapered hole.
  - Barrel-shaped hole.

- [ ] General rules for using attribute gages:
  - Holding the gage in one hand and the part in the other is the preferred method of using attribute gages.
  - If the gage and/or the part is heavier than can be lifted easily, apply the lighter of the two (gage or part) to the other.

# Using Attribute Gages - cont.

- [ ] To use plug and ring gages:
  - Select a plug gage of the correct size and tolerance for the hole being checked.
  - Clean both ends of the gage and the hole in the workpiece with a clean, dry cloth.
  - Wipe both ends of the gage with an oily cloth to deposit a thin film of oil on the surfaces.
  - Start the "go" gage squarely into the hole. If the hole is within the limits, the gage will enter easily. Do not force or turn it. The plug should enter the hole for the full length, and there should be no excessive play between the plug and the part.
  - After the hole has been checked with the "go" gage, it should be checked with the "no/go" end. This gage should not begin to enter the hole. Any entry of more than 1/16 in. (1.58 mm) indicates an oversize, bell-mouth, or tapered hole.

- [ ] Care of plug and ring gages:
  - Store gages in divided wooden trays to protect them from being nicked or burred.
  - Check them frequently for size and accuracy.
  - Correctly align gages with the workpiece to prevent binding.
  - Do not force or twist a plain plug or ring gage. Forcing or twisting will cause excessive wear.
  - Clean the gage and workpiece thoroughly before checking the part.
  - Use a light film of oil on the gage to help prevent binding.
  - Make provision for air to escape when gaging blind holes with a plug gage.
  - Have gages and work at room temperature to ensure accuracy and prevent damage to the gage.
  - Never use an inspection gage as a working gage.

- [ ] To use a flush pin gage:
  - Clean both the part and the gage.
  - Apply the part to the gage.
  - The pin must stop between the steps on the gage. If it does not, the part is either too long or too short.

# Defect Identification

Identifying defects seems like an easy job. You simply check the part using gages and measuring tools. Then you check it visually for problems. Easy, right? Well... not really. On the surface it seems straight-forward enough, but there are many obstacles that you must overcome. These include:

- [ ] **Gray areas**
  Not all characteristics are easy to define. What does *"no burrs"* mean? What does *"no marks"* mean? There are many like this as well, where judgement and interpretation enter in to the decision making process.

- [ ] **Individual interpretations**
  Each person may have their own interpretation about what is a defect and what is not. Often, this leads to disagreements which can't be resolved easily.

- [ ] **False sense of security.**
  Most defects are visual in nature. When a press is running well, we tend to let our guard down. Visual defects are difficult to catch when our guard is down because there are no reminders which force us to check for each visual defect possibility. With gages and measuring tools, there are at least reminders that the listed dimensions must be checked. This is still not enough all of the time, though.

- [ ] **Workload**
  There are times when our workload rises to a point where quality checks are not completed as thoroughly as they should be. Also, when work load decreases we tend to let our guard down, thinking that we have time to catch everything.

How can we overcome these obstacles? Here are some tips that could help identify defects and problems:

- [ ] **Prevention**
  The key to reducing defects and problems is to prevent them from occurring. This means we must anticipate what will happen. The best early warning sign is when something changes. Anytime something changes you should be asking questions like *"will this continue?"* and if it will continue, *"what will happen if this change continues?"*. Always try to anticipate problems so that they can be prevented.

- [ ] **Quantify everything**
  This means that we must eliminate all gray areas and individual interpretations. Put numbers and specific examples in place of abstract descriptions. Example - *"no burrs allowed on rim edge"* could be changed to *".002 burr maximum"* and checked with an indicator or calipers.

- [ ] **Stay Alert**
  Always be paranoid about each part you pick up. This doesn't mean that you *"look for things that aren't there"*, but rather, that you stay alert for problems. Usually they appear when you least expect them.

# Defect Identification - cont.

- [ ] **Check the** *"inside of the part first, then the outside"*

- [ ] **Check for visual problems first, then move over to the measuring tools and gauges.**

- [ ] **Ask for help**
  When the workload skyrockets because more presses are running or because people are on vacation, ask for help. Running bad parts because of a high workload makes less sense than not running the press in the first place. Many times, just having someone to do small things like watching coolant levels and changing coils can free up enough of your time to do good quality checks, while not placing too great of a burden on those helping. When you off-load work, remember to off-load relatively simple tasks like watching coolant levels, changing coils, etc. People will be more willing to help you if the job doesn't consume all of their time and it is simple.

- [ ] **Make up your mind**
  If you think that you have a defect, you probably do. In the best case scenario you probably have a future defect. So you might as well deal with it now. But you must make up your own mind after collecting facts about whether the part is good or bad.

- [ ] **Use effective Interpersonal Skills**
  Use effective interpersonal skills to solve problems. Tips include:
  - Attack the problem, not the person.
  - Remember, you both want to solve the problem. Key in on that, not on who's right or wrong.
  - Use active listening to truly understand the other person's point of view.
  - Separate the message being sent from the way in which the message is sent. For example, sometimes what a person has to say is correct, but they are very angry when they say it. It's difficult, but respond to what they're saying, not the anger. This will diffuse the situation.
  - Treat others with courtesy and professionalism.

- [ ] **Ask for a Mediator**
  There are times when you don't agree with someone else. Use effective interpersonal skills to solve the problem. If that doesn't do it, ask for someone impartial to make the call. This person could be a toolmaker, Quality Manager, Quality Engineer, or your Supervisor.

# Defect Identification - cont.

So, what types of defects do you look for? Here are some common ones:

- [ ] **Characteristic doesn't meet specification**
  This defect can be found with measuring instruments or gages.

- [ ] **Visual defect**
  This could include score marks, burrs, missing features (like holes, tabs, etc.) or anything that is visually different or missing.

- [ ] **Statistical change**
  This can be found when using SPC charts.
