# Materials

The term "*materials*" refers to the raw material used in the stamping process. It also includes the scrap generated during this process. In this module you'll learn about material types, dumping scrap, and hanging coils.

# Types of Material

Most of the material we use falls into one of five categories. The materials we use are:

- [ ] Cold Rolled Steel (CRS)
- [ ] Stainless Steel (SS)
- [ ] Brass
- [ ] Aluminum
- [ ] Copper

The type of material being used for the job at the press can be identified by looking at the production schedule. Always double-check the material type on the production schedule or by asking your Supervisor.

# Material Defects

As you handle raw material you must stay alert to the problems you might encounter. There are a number of visual defects to watch for, including:

- [ ] **Tape** - tape is used at the end of the coil to fasten the coil end to the coil itself. Always remove all type and cut off the damaged end of the coil.
- [ ] **Rust** - rust occurs when a coil is brought into contact with moisture. Material with heavy rust cannot be used because it shows up on the finished part.
- [ ] **Cardboard spacers** - spacers are used in the coil to wind it up evenly. They may stick to the bottom side of the coil. These are tough to spot, but if you do, if will save many hours of tool maintenance.
- [ ] **Damaged edges** - these are easy to spot on the coil. Cut out spots that won't pass through the blank die.
- [ ] **Lamination** - this defect is difficult to spot because it is a defect found under the surface of the material. It sometimes causes the surface to peel.
- [ ] **Welds** - if a coil is joined together in the middle of a coil, it is usually welded. Welds can cause severe damage.
- [ ] **Scratches** - a scratch or gouge is normally caused by the material scraping across something sharp or dragging the floor.
- [ ] **Paper interleaving** - paper is sometimes used on stainless steel to prevent scratches. This paper separates each coil wrap. It must not be allowed to get into the press.

These defects are difficult to see while the press is running. Keep a close eye on the material entering the press. If you do see a visual defect try to shut down the press before the defect reaches the tooling. This could save many hours of tool maintenance.

# Scrap Removal

Scrap removal means taking scrap generated at the press and dumping it into the correct container or hopper. The following steps should be followed when dumping scrap:

- [ ] Identify the material.
  - Always refer to the blueprint or the raw material tag for the material type you want to dump.
  - Never assume that you know what material type you are looking at.
  - If you don't know, ask.

- [ ] Identify the container.
  - Each different type of material is dumped into separate containers. This also means, for example, that the various types of stainless may not be mixed. See your Supervisor for further information on what material types must be kept separate.
  - Always use a clean container. Never use a contaminated container. A few shavings of CRS in a brass scrap container could cause thousands of dollars in sorting.
  - If you don't know where to dump the material, ask.

- [ ] Never mix materials.
  - Always verify what you have before dumping. Mixed materials must always be sorted.

- [ ] Dump the scrap using safe procedures.
  - If it is too heavy, get help.
  - Always wear gloves.

# Scrap Report

A scrap report is a record of the parts scrapped at the press while a job is running. This must be tracked so that we can measure our scrap rate. Our goal is to decrease scrap and the place to begin is by measuring it.

One of your jobs is to weigh up the scrap at the press each day, fill out a scrap report, and dispose of the scrap appropriately. See your Supervisor for the specifics of filling out a scrap report.

# Overhead Crane Operation

You must be a licensed crane operator to operate an overhead crane. If you are not licensed, you may not operate the crane. Improper crane operation can result in serious injury. If you are a licensed crane operator, let's review some basic crane techniques for lifting coils. To use an overhead crane correctly*, use these procedures:

- You must immediately report any problems or defects in a crane to your supervisor.
- If the top of the load is not lifted to a height greater than five (5) feet, the load is not considered an overhead hazard. Overhead hazards require the use of hard hats.
- Never lift more than the rated capacity of the crane.
- When a load approaches the rated load, you must test the hoisting brake by raising the load a few inches and applying the brakes.
- Never lower a load below a point where less than two full wraps of wire rope remain on the hoisting drum.
- When a power failure occurs, always place all controllers in the OFF position.
- If you leave the crane unattended, always land any attached load and place the controllers in the OFF position.
- Where there is doubt about a crane's safety, stop the crane immediately and report the condition to your supervisor.
- You must respond to signals only from the associate directing the lift. However, you must always obey AT ALL TIMES an emergency stop signal from any associate.
- Never carry a load over another person.
- Never use the crane to make a side pull.
- Never ride the hoisting device like a magnet, hook, chain, strap, ball, or load.
- When attaching or moving a load, you must make sure:
  - The hoisting strap or chain is free of kinks or twists and not wrapped around the load.
  - The load is attached to a load block hook by means of a sling or other approved device.
  - The sling and load will clear all obstacles or obstructions.
  - The load is balanced and secured before lifting the load more than a few inches.
  - That multiple lines are not twisted around each other.
  - The hook is brought over the load in a manner to prevent swinging.
  - There is not sudden acceleration or deceleration of the moving load.

*This is a partial list of crane operation rules. Again, never operate a crane unless you are licensed to do so.

# Stock Reels

A stock reel is a motorized device used to unwrap material from the coil. It unwraps the correct amount of material as the press uses it. There are two different types of stock reels. One type can accept only one coil at a time and the other type can handle multiple coils. Here are some details on each.

**Single Coil**

With a single coil stock reel you must pick up each coil individually and place it on the reel. Each of these reels have the following basic features:

Mandrel - Each coil of material is slipped onto the mandrel. The mandrel is made up of three arms which expand outward to apply pressure to the inside of the coil to hold it in place.

Keepers - Coil keepers are slipped onto each pad of the mandrel to keep outer wraps of the stock from falling off the coil sideways.

Operator controls - Operator controls include a switch which changes the coil between "*manual*" and "*automatic*" modes, a forward/reverse switch to change between "*forward*" and "*reverse*" rotation, and a button for manual rotation of the mandrel.

Loop control - To keep a smooth delivery of raw material going into the press, a "*loop*" of material is created between the reel and the press. This "*loop*" provides a buffer between the reel "*paying out*" material and the press using it up as it runs. The unit that maintains this buffer is the "*loop control*" and is usually an arm that is automatically controlled.

**Multiple Coil**

A multiple coil reel is called a pallet dereeler. It allows you to place an entire pallet of material on the reel at one time. This eliminates the need to place each coil on separately. The operation of a pallet dereeler is different in that it uses a rotating table rather than a mandrel with keepers. While the loop control looks different, it actually serves the same purpose as a standard stock reel.

See your Supervisor for more information about the stock reels we use and how to operate them.

# Hang a Coil

### WARNING:

Performing the skills outline in this section require operating a crane and/or a hi-lo. YOU MUST BE LICENSED BY YOUR CURRENT COMPANY BEFORE OPERATING EITHER OF THESE UNITS. EACH UNIT REQUIRES A SEPARATE LICENSE.

The steps involved in hanging a coil include the following:

- [ ] Identify the coil to be used.
- [ ] Remove coil from the skid.
- [ ] Hang coil on stock reel.
- [ ] Feed the coil strip through the press.
- [ ] Restart the press.

### Step #1: Identify the coil to be used.

- Identify the coil by matching the production schedule requirement to the tag attached to the coil. Serious problems could result from using the wrong coil.
- Material type, thickness, and width as well as the job number must be identified.

### Step #2: Remove coil from the skid.

- If the space between coils is too small for a sling to slide through, pry the coil up carefully using the tip of a Hi-Lo fork or a pry bar. After prying it up, place a spacer between the coils, then slide the sling through.
- Start with the hook directly above the point where you're picking up the coil with the sling. Then, while picking up one side of the coil, observe the point where the coil still rests on the skid. The cable of the crane should be moved as you lift so that it is directly above this point. Continue picking up the coil. Using this trick will cut down on coil swing once it is free of the skid. If you pick up the coil with the cable directly above the original pick-up point the coil will swing violently, possibly causing damage or injury.

### Step #3: Hang coil on stock reel.

- Turn the coil so that the stock will feed from the top of the coil.
- Position coil fully against the back side of reel after adjusting the mandrel small enough to accept the coil.
- Lower the coil onto the expandable pads. Unhook the sling from the crane hook.
- Remove the sling by turning the reel until the sling is on bottom. Move the crane to a neutral spot.
- Expand the mandrel until the internal diameter of the coil is gripped tightly. Do not drop the wrench on the floor - this causes chips in the cement.
- Slide the keepers in place. Always use them to keep the coil from unraveling to the side.

# Hang a Coil - cont.

### Step #4: Feed the coil strip through the press.

- Use the following precautions when unbanding coils:
  - Always remove radial bands first, and O.D. retaining bands last.
  - Never stand in a danger zone which includes any area where a band or part of a coil could snap upon release.
  - Always place the end of the coil downward so that when it is released it does not snap into the aisle.
  - Never pull on a band that is stuck. It could suddenly come out and snap against your arm, causing injury. Instead, cut it into pieces.
  - Always use gloves, and where appropriate, kevlar sleeves.
- Carefully feed the strip under the roller while staying clear of the turning reel.
- Continue pulling on the loose end of the coil as the stock reel uncoils material.
- Feed the end through the press which includes the feed rollers, the blank, and the scrap chopper.

### Step #5: Restart the press.

- We will cover this in the next module. Do not restart a press until you have completed module six or have been specifically and certified trained to perform this function.
